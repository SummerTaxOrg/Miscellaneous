# 02 — Voice and Substance: Adapting the Cause to the Op-Ed

The instruction was to follow "the same styling of writing." This document records what was
kept, what was changed, and where each argument in the op-ed comes from — so the piece is a
faithful member of the family that includes the memo, the speech, and the brief, not a
restatement of any one of them.

## I. What "the same styling" means across four documents

All four works share one discovered insight: **the Massachusetts Declaration of Rights that
the case rests on was drafted by John Adams to secure the home and to forbid taxation
without consent.** Each work expresses it in the register its audience requires:

| Document | Audience | History | Register |
|---|---|---|---|
| Memo | the lawyer / file | analyzed, with case law | candid, doctrinal |
| Speech | the public, aloud | invoked **obliquely**, through cadence; never named | oratorical, pervasive patriotism |
| Amicus brief | the SJC | argued **openly**, by citation (original meaning) | disciplined, patriotism rationed |
| **Op-ed** | newspaper readers | named **once, lightly**, for authority and warmth | plain-spoken, first person; cadence at the edges |

The op-ed sits closest to the speech in *spirit* (a citizen's patriotism) but closest to the
brief in *honesty* (it concedes the shared-burden point). Its distinctive move is the
**first person with firsthand standing** — the one voice none of the other three documents
could use.

## II. Where each paragraph's substance comes from

Every claim in the op-ed traces to the underlying research, so the piece is persuasive
without overstating:

- **"I am a person trying to get my kids under a roof… nothing to flip"** ← the memo's
  owner-builder profile and the clean-standing posture (memo Parts 1.B, 3.B).
- **"6.25 percent… on the receipt… owed as a 'use tax'"** ← the tax mechanics (memo Part 1.B;
  G.L. c. 64H/64I).
- **"framing lumber or a window or a spool of wire"** ← the property-right-core framing
  shared with the speech and brief (memo Part 4.B.1), but deliberately reworded with fresh,
  specific nouns rather than the siblings' recurring "lumber, nail, beam, glass" image.
- **"acquire and protect property" / "without the consent of the people, or their
  representatives"** ← Decl. of Rights arts. 1 and 23 (the consent clause quoted once,
  lightly, for resonance).
- **"When John Adams sat down in 1780 to draft the Massachusetts constitution"** ← the
  historical research behind the speech and brief (SPEECH/research/01; AMICUS/research/03).
- **"an exemption, reportedly worth something like thirty-five million dollars annually…
  multifamily"** ← memo Part 1.D; used here, as in the brief, to show the line against the
  family home is arbitrary ("both come off the same truck").
- **"the state's own rules treat the builder as the 'consumer' of whatever he installs"** ←
  the contractor-as-consumer rule (Directive 02-16; LR 80-44; 830 CMR 64H.1.1), the same
  fact that grounds the amici's standing (AMICUS/research/02).
- **"the smallest charges tend to be the ones nobody bothers to question"** ← the Thoreauvian
  theme from the speech ("the small coin of a thousand receipts"), but rewritten in the
  op-ed's own words rather than reusing the speech's image.
- **"I have asked a court to weigh whether…"** ← the c. 231A declaratory-judgment vehicle
  (memo Part 2). *Flagged as a placeholder: true only if the actual author has in fact filed.*

## III. Honest limits, kept in view

The op-ed makes the strongest fair case, but the cause's candor is preserved structurally:

- It **concedes the shared burden** outright ("I am not asking to dodge my share"), mirroring
  the memo's and brief's acknowledgment that arts. 10/23 *authorize* consented taxation.
- It claims the tax is wrong **in principle and unequal in practice** — it does *not* promise
  the author will win, and it routes the remedy to "a courtroom or… the State House," leaving
  the legal odds (which the memo treats candidly) outside the persuasive frame.
- It avoids the forbidden overclaim the brief and speech also avoid: it never equates a sales
  tax with revolutionary-era tyranny. It claims only that the *principle* the founders wrote
  down still draws a line, and that this tax crosses it.

The result is meant to read as what it is: a real person, building a real house, making a
principled argument his neighbors can recognize — and, behind it, the same constitutional
idea the other three documents carry, in the voice the opinion page rewards.
