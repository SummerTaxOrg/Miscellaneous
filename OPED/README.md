# OP-ED — "I'm Building My Family a Home. The State Taxes Me for the Privilege."

This directory holds the formatting research and the notes on voice behind the op-ed at
[`/OP-ED.md`](../OP-ED.md).

## What this is

The fourth work product on a single cause. The
[memo](../MA-owner-builder-sales-tax-constitutional-memo.md) is the candid legal analysis;
the [speech](../SPEECH.md) is the public, patriotic voice; the
[amicus brief](../AMICUS-BRIEF.md) is the organized ally in court. **This is the citizen's
voice in the newspaper** — a short, first-person opinion piece by an individual owner-builder
making the case to ordinary readers that the materials of a family's home should not bear
the sales tax.

## The client's request

> Based on this, and following the same styling of writing, prepare an op-ed piece written
> by an individual supporting this set of ideals.

## How it keeps "the same styling" while changing form

The op-ed inherits the cause's soul — the original-meaning, consent-to-taxation argument and
the patriotic register established in the speech and brief — but it is rebuilt to the
discipline of the opinion page: **first person, firsthand experience, ~750 words, plain and
pithy, one clean argument, a concession to the other side, and a call to action.** The
elevated, founding-era cadence is rationed to the open and the close; the middle does
concrete, conversational work. See [`research/02-voice-and-substance.md`](research/02-voice-and-substance.md).

## Caveats

- **Placeholders.** The byline, hometown, and the author's identifying line are bracketed —
  an op-ed's persuasive power depends on a *real* author with firsthand standing, which is
  exactly the owner-builder profile the [memo](../MA-owner-builder-sales-tax-constitutional-memo.md)
  builds its case around. Fill these in before submitting.
- **Verify figures.** The ~$35M 2026 multifamily exemption and any litigation status
  referenced ("I've filed…") must be confirmed true of the actual author before publication.
- **Advocacy, not advice.** Like its companions, the op-ed argues the cause at its most
  persuasive and openly concedes the shared-burden point; the memo remains the honest
  forecast of the legal odds.
