# The Declaration from the Market Steps — Annotated

The clean, performable text lives at [`/SPEECH.md`](../../SPEECH.md). This copy exposes the
seams: each passage is followed by a note identifying the tradition it draws on (see
[`research/01`](../research/01-the-two-traditions.md)) and the work it does for the cause
(see [`research/03`](../research/03-from-memo-to-oration.md)).

Legend: **[A]** = founding-era / Adams & the Declaration form · **[T]** = Thoreau /
*Civil Disobedience* · **[P]** = place / setting · **[L]** = load-bearing legal point.

---

> When in the long course of a free people's life it becomes necessary for one among them
> to stand upon the public stones and speak plainly of a wrong — not the loud wrong of
> armies and proclamations, but the quiet wrong that enters a household by the door of its
> own purse — then a decent respect for the judgment of his neighbors requires that he
> declare the causes which move him to speak.

**[A]** The Declaration's opening silhouette ("When in the course of human events… a
decent respect to the opinions of mankind"), rebuilt from new words so it is *felt*, not
quoted. **[A]** The antithesis "loud wrong of armies / quiet wrong… by the door of its
own purse" pre-empts the objection that a sales tax is too small to merit such language —
it reframes scale as *stealth*.

> We hold these things to be plain, and in need of no man's permission to be true. That
> every soul is born possessed of itself, and of the labor of its own two hands…

**[A]** "We hold these…" — the grammar of self-evident truth. **[L]** Art. 1's natural
right of "acquiring, possessing, and protecting property," stated as axiom rather than
citation.

> …to acquire by honest toil, to possess in quiet, and to protect from the reach of any
> power whatsoever the roof beneath which one lays down one's children to sleep.

**[A]** Tricolon (acquire / possess / protect) — lifted in *structure* from Art. 1.
**[L]** The memo's key move (the property right is "most concretely exercised in building
and owning a home") rendered as the image of a sleeping child under a roof.

> That governments are raised among us to guard these very things, and draw their whole
> authority from the consent of the governed; and that whenever a power forgets the
> purpose of its making… the people are not bound to silence.

**[A]** Consent-of-the-governed and the right of redress — the Declaration's political
theory, stated in the founders' cadence.

> There is nothing a person holds more nearly than the walls he has raised with his own
> hands… the body's second skin, the family's first country…

**[L]** The home as the core of the property interest. **[A/T]** The metaphors do the work
an adjective would spoil.

> To tax the silk and the spice, the carriage and the ornament — of this men have always
> grumbled and always borne it… But the timber before it is a wall, the nail, the beam,
> the pane of glass against the winter — these are not the furniture of plenty… shelter
> itself, taken apart and laid out in pieces.

**[A]** Antithesis (luxury vs. necessity) drawn out across a periodic sentence. **[L]**
The memo's distinction between taxing ordinary commodities and taxing the *inputs to
shelter* — the heart of the as-applied theory.

> Hear, too, the oldest and simplest of our principles… that no charge, toll, tax,
> impost, or exaction of any name whatsoever is justly laid upon a free people but by
> their own consent, or the consent of those whom they have sent to speak in their place.

**[L]** Art. 23 / Art. 10, the consent-to-taxation clause — nearly its own words
("subsidy, charge, tax, impost, or duties… without the consent of the people, or their
representatives"), recast as oratory. **[A]** Polysyndeton ("toll, tax, impost, or
exaction") for the gravity of an enumerated list.

> We do not deny the common burden. Every soul that is protected owes its share to the
> protection. But consent to the general burden of the state was never… a consent to be
> taxed for the act of building one's own shelter…

**[L]** The crucial concession. The memo's Part 4.C admits Art. 10 obliges each person to
"contribute his share"; the speech *concedes it openly* and then draws the narrow line —
the tax exceeds the boundary, taxation itself does not offend. Conceding the strong
counter-argument is itself a founding-era forensic habit, and it makes the listener
trust the speaker.

> Let the particulars be set down, that they may be weighed by plain men:

**[A]** The hinge into the bill of grievances — the Declaration's "He has…" indictment,
here "It has…" against the taxing power. Anaphora turns a list into an accumulating
verdict.

> — It has laid its hand upon the materials of a dwelling before ever they became a
> dwelling…
> — It has taxed the beam and the board, the nail and the glass…
> — It has drawn no honest line between the speculator who builds to sell at a profit and
> the father who builds only to live…
> — It has found grace and exemption for many a structure raised by other hands… yet has
> found none for the single roof a person raises over the heads of his own household.
> — And it has called this exaction a contribution to the common protection, while…it has
> protected nothing under heaven but its own revenue.

**[L]** Five grievances, each a memo point: (1) tax on materials qua materials; (2) the
necessities of shelter; (3) the owner-occupant vs. speculator distinction (Part 3.B); (4)
the exemptions that reach other projects but not the owner-builder (Parts 1.C–D); (5) the
public-purpose / confiscation theory (Part 4.B.4). **[A]** Sustained anaphora is the
Declaration's engine of indignation.

> Now let me speak more nearly, as one man speaking to his own conscience…

**[T] — THE TURN.** The structural pivot of the whole speech: the public *we* yields to
the private *I*. This is the move that makes it Thoreau's and not only Adams's.

> The state is a great machine, and most of us, most of the time, are content to be its
> smooth and frictionless parts… We pay for the road… the school and the bridge and the
> watch kept through the night, and we are right to.

**[T]** Thoreau's machine, and his concession that the machine does good — disarming the
charge of anti-government zeal, and again conceding the just tax before objecting to the
unjust one.

> But there comes at last an exaction of a different kind… that asks me to buy back from
> the public treasury the right to do the most natural and most private thing a free
> creature ever does, which is to raise itself a home. To a demand of that kind a
> person's conscience may quietly answer: no. And he may stand upon that no, and let the
> machine feel that he is there.

**[T]** Thoreau's "counter-friction to stop the machine," paraphrased so it is an echo,
not a quotation. **[T]** The aphoristic short sentence ("may quietly answer: no") lands
the moral point against the surrounding grand periods.

> I counsel no tumult, and I throw no tea into no harbor.

**[P/T]** The single line that comes *nearest* the forbidden direct reference — and stops
short, as a *disavowal*. It gestures at the harbor's memory while explicitly refusing the
comparison, which both honors the discipline of [`research/02`](../research/02-the-spirit-of-the-place.md)
and keeps the cause peaceable. **[L]** It also signals the lawful posture the memo
requires.

> I counsel only this: that a free person remember he was a person before ever he was a
> subject, and a conscience before ever he was a taxpayer; that he seek his redress in the
> open court and by the lawful and reasoned word…

**[T]** Conscience prior to citizenship — the spine of *Civil Disobedience*. **[L]**
"Redress in the open court" ties the moral stance to the memo's declaratory-judgment
vehicle (Part 2) — refusal expressed *through law*, not against it.

> …and that he refuse, gently and immovably, to let so great a right be carried off in so
> small a coin — in the little forgettable sum at the bottom of a thousand receipts, where
> tyranny, when it is wise, has always preferred to hide.

**[T]** Thoreau's insight that injustice is usually quiet and administrative. The
"thousand receipts" returns us to the opening's "wrong… by the door of its own purse,"
closing the frame.

> And so, upon these public stones, beneath a dome that a thrifty people once raised for
> themselves without laying upon their neighbors a single new tax to do it, in the salt
> wind of a harbor that has carried into this city both honest cargo and unbending
> conviction — we do solemnly publish and declare:

**[P]** The full permitted budget of *place*, all at once: the granite steps; the true
fact that Josiah Quincy built this market without a new tax (irony, not comparison); the
harbor of "cargo and conviction" (trade and rebellion) without naming an event. **[A]**
"We do solemnly publish and declare" — the Declaration's performative close; the *we*
returns.

> That the right to acquire, to possess, and to protect a home is a natural and
> unalienable right…
> That a free people may be asked to share the common burden, but may not be made to
> purchase, at their own thresholds, the leave to shelter their own;
> That where consent is the only just foundation of a tax, an exaction… wants that
> foundation;
> And that we will seek the redress of this wrong by every lawful means a free
> commonwealth affords…

**[A]** The resolved clauses of a declaration ("That… That… That…"). **[L]** Each clause
is a memo holding compressed to a creed: the Art. 1 right; the boundary on consented
taxation; the Art. 23 consent foundation; the lawful (c. 231A) redress.

> We pledge it to one another, and to those who will build their homes long after we have
> laid down our tools…
> **A home is the last fortress of a free people, and they will not be taxed upon its
> threshold without their leave.**

**[A]** The mutual pledge ("we mutually pledge to each other…") — the Declaration's final
gesture — extended to *posterity* (those who build after). **[A/T]** The closing maxim is
built to be quoted alone: the founders' aphoristic finish, carrying the consent principle
(Art. 23) in nine words.
