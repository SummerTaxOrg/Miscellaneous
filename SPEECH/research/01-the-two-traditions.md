# 01 — The Two Traditions, and What the Speech Borrows From Each

The commission names two voices a century apart: the founding-era arguer (John Adams and
his peers) and the Transcendentalist essayist (Thoreau's *Civil Disobedience*). They are
not interchangeable, and the speech does not blend them into a single grey tone. It
**moves between them** — opening and closing in the founders' public, declarative
register, and turning at its center into Thoreau's private, conscientious one. Below is
the evaluation of each tradition and the specific machinery drawn from it.

---

## I. The founding-era arguer — John Adams and the Declaration form

### Why Adams, specifically

The choice of Adams is not decorative. The constitutional theory in the memo rests on the
**Massachusetts Declaration of Rights of 1780 — and John Adams was its principal
draftsman.** Articles 1, 10, and 23, which the brief leans on most heavily, came from his
pen. To write "in the style of John Adams" is therefore to return the argument to the
voice of the man who wrote the very words being invoked. This is the deepest and least
visible of the speech's resonances: it is not an allusion, it is an inheritance.

Adams the writer was, first, a *lawyer* — trained to marshal a case, to anticipate the
opposing argument, to reason from first principles to a particular conclusion. His public
prose (the *Novanglus* letters, *Thoughts on Government*, the 1780 Constitution's
preamble) is declarative, periodic, morally certain, and built on the conviction that a
government is a thing *made by men for stated purposes* and answerable to them.

### Devices borrowed from this tradition

| Device | How it appears in the speech |
|---|---|
| **The Declaration form itself** | Preamble of occasion → self-evident truths → an indictment in parallel clauses → a solemn published declaration → a mutual pledge. The whole oration is shaped to the silhouette of the 1776 Declaration, so the ear recognizes the *form* without being told. |
| **"A decent respect to the opinions of mankind"** | Opened as *"a decent respect for the judgment of his neighbors requires that he declare the causes."* The cadence is the founders'; the words are not quoted. |
| **Self-evident truths** | *"We hold these things to be plain, and in need of no man's permission to be true."* The grammar of axiom — assertions presented as needing no proof. |
| **The bill of grievances** | The catalogue of "It has…" clauses mirrors the Declaration's "He has…" indictment of the King. Anaphora makes a list feel like an accumulating verdict. |
| **First-principles reasoning** | Natural right → purpose of government → consent as the foundation of taxation → the particular wrong. The lawyer's syllogism dressed as oratory. |
| **Periodic sentence & tricolon** | Long sentences that suspend their meaning to a final clause; the rule of three (*"to acquire by honest toil, to possess in quiet, and to protect…"*). |
| **The solemn performative close** | *"We do solemnly publish and declare"* — language that *enacts* rather than merely describes, the founders' favorite closing gesture. |

## II. The Transcendentalist conscience — Thoreau's *Civil Disobedience*

### What Thoreau contributes that Adams cannot

Adams argues about *the people* and *the state* — the public, plural, institutional
plane. Thoreau argues about **the single soul standing alone against the machine.** His
1849 essay (delivered first as a lecture, then published as *Resistance to Civil
Government*) grew from his own refusal to pay a poll tax and the night he spent in jail
for it. Its register is private, plain, aphoristic, and morally interior. It supplies the
one thing the Declaration form lacks: the *I*, the conscience, the lonely act of refusal.

The fit to this particular cause is almost uncanny. Thoreau's whole essay turns on **a tax
a free conscience cannot in good faith pay.** A speech about a tax on the materials of
one's own home is, at its heart, a Thoreauvian problem before it is a constitutional one.

### Devices borrowed from this tradition

| Device | How it appears in the speech |
|---|---|
| **The turn to the first person** | The speech's center pivots: *"Now let me speak more nearly, as one man speaking to his own conscience."* The public *we* yields to the private *I* — the structural heartbeat of *Civil Disobedience*. |
| **The machine metaphor** | Thoreau: men serve the state as machines, and a just man must sometimes be "a counter-friction to stop the machine." Echoed as *"the state is a great machine… let the machine feel that he is there"* — without quoting the line. |
| **The conscience above the law** | *"A person was a person before ever he was a subject, and a conscience before ever he was a taxpayer."* Pure Thoreau: the individual's moral judgment is prior to the citizen's legal duty. |
| **The qualified, peaceable refusal** | Thoreau resists but does not riot. The speech is careful: *"I counsel no tumult… I counsel only this."* Refusal as moral stance, not insurrection — which also keeps the cause inside the lawful, declaratory posture the memo requires. |
| **Aphoristic plainness** | Against the founders' grand periods, short blunt sentences land the moral point: *"To a demand of that kind a person's conscience may quietly answer: no."* |
| **The small sum where tyranny hides** | Thoreau's insight that injustice is often administrative and quiet, not dramatic, becomes *"the little forgettable sum at the bottom of a thousand receipts, where tyranny, when it is wise, has always preferred to hide."* |

---

## III. How the two are joined

The seam is deliberate and is the speech's central craft decision:

```
EXORDIUM + FIRST PRINCIPLES  ──────────  Adams / Declaration (public "we")
   the home as natural right
   consent as the foundation of a tax
THE INDICTMENT  ───────────────────────  Adams / Declaration (the "It has…" bill)
        ↓  [THE TURN]
THE CONSCIENCE  ───────────────────────  Thoreau (private "I", the machine, refusal)
        ↓  [THE RETURN]
PERORATION + PLEDGE  ──────────────────  Adams / Declaration (public "we" restored)
```

The founders' frame gives the cause its *authority and scale*; Thoreau's interior gives
it its *intimacy and moral courage*. The listener is moved from "this is our common
right" to "this is my own conscience" and back to "this we pledge together" — the arc
that turns a legal grievance into a patriotic one.
