# SPEECH — "The Declaration from the Market Steps"

This directory holds the research, evaluation, and craft annotations behind the oration
at [`/SPEECH.md`](../SPEECH.md).

## What this is

The repository's research memo
([`MA-owner-builder-sales-tax-constitutional-memo.md`](../MA-owner-builder-sales-tax-constitutional-memo.md))
builds a constitutional challenge to the Massachusetts sales/use tax as imposed on the
home-construction materials an individual buys to build his own house. Its lead hook is
the **Massachusetts Declaration of Rights** — the natural right to *acquire, possess, and
protect property* (Art. 1), the *consent-to-taxation* and *due-process* guarantees
(Arts. 10, 12), and the rule that no tax may be laid *"without the consent of the people,
or their representatives"* (Art. 23).

The brief is the argument made to a court. **The speech is that same argument made to a
people** — recast as a public oration in the manner of "a Declaration of Independence
read from the steps of Quincy Market": grounded in the legal theory, but reaching for the
moral and patriotic register rather than the doctrinal one.

## The commission, in the client's words

> A speech that would be the equivalent of a Declaration of Independence read from the
> steps of Quincy Market, invoking the ethereal deep history of the setting — *not* by
> directly referencing or comparing to it, but through a deeper understanding of what the
> style of speech can produce and invoke in the nostalgic patriotism of an American;
> written in the styles of the great arguers of the founding era, such as **John Adams**,
> and carrying the deep-seated style of the great American Transcendentalist essays, such
> as **Henry David Thoreau's** *Civil Disobedience*.

## How to read it

1. Start with the oration at [`/SPEECH.md`](../SPEECH.md) — read it aloud; it is built for the ear.
2. [`research/01`](research/01-the-two-traditions.md) explains *why it sounds the way it does*.
3. [`research/02`](research/02-the-spirit-of-the-place.md) explains *why the setting is the right one*, and the discipline of invoking it without naming it.
4. [`research/03`](research/03-from-memo-to-oration.md) shows the *seam* between law and rhetoric — what each line is doing for the legal cause.
5. [`oration/`](oration/declaration-of-the-owner-builder--annotated.md) is the speech with the seams exposed.

## A standing caution

This is rhetoric in service of a cause, not a legal instrument. The memo it springs from
is candid that the constitutional theory faces real doctrinal headwinds (the same
Articles 10 and 23 that protect property *also authorize* legislatively-consented
taxation; a flat statewide excise draws deferential review). The speech makes the
people's case at its most stirring; the memo keeps the lawyer's honesty. Keep both in
view, and do not mistake the one for the other.
