# THE DECLARATION FROM THE MARKET STEPS

### An Oration upon the Right of a Free People to Build, to Possess, and to Withhold Their Consent

*To be delivered upon the granite steps of the public market, in the open air, before the assembled people.*

---

When in the long course of a free people's life it becomes necessary for one among
them to stand upon the public stones and speak plainly of a wrong — not the loud
wrong of armies and proclamations, but the quiet wrong that enters a household by the
door of its own purse — then a decent respect for the judgment of his neighbors
requires that he declare the causes which move him to speak, and lay them open, that
every honest mind may weigh them.

We hold these things to be plain, and in need of no man's permission to be true. That
every soul is born possessed of itself, and of the labor of its own two hands. That
among the first and least deniable of the rights so given is this: to acquire by
honest toil, to possess in quiet, and to protect from the reach of any power
whatsoever the roof beneath which one lays down one's children to sleep. That
governments are raised among us to guard these very things, and draw their whole
authority from the consent of the governed; and that whenever a power forgets the
purpose of its making, and turns its instruments against the very rights it was made
to keep, the people are not bound to silence.

Consider, then, what manner of possession a home is. There is nothing a person holds
more nearly than the walls he has raised with his own hands. A dwelling so built is
not merchandise upon a shelf; it is the body's second skin, the family's first
country, the one small province of the earth where a free creature may at last shut
the door and be sovereign. To tax the silk and the spice, the carriage and the
ornament — of this men have always grumbled and always borne it, for these are the
furniture of plenty. But the timber before it is a wall, the nail, the beam, the pane
of glass against the winter — these are not the furniture of plenty. They are the
plainest necessities of the human condition. They are shelter itself, taken apart and
laid out in pieces. And a power that meets a man at the threshold of his own unfinished
home, and makes him purchase, coin by coin, the bare privilege of sheltering his own,
has confused the price of luxury with the price of existence.

Hear, too, the oldest and simplest of our principles, which time has not worn thin: that
no charge, toll, tax, impost, or exaction of any name whatsoever is justly laid upon a
free people but by their own consent, or the consent of those whom they have sent to
speak in their place. We do not deny the common burden. Every soul that is protected
owes its share to the protection. But consent to the general burden of the state was
never, and could never be, a consent to be taxed for the act of building one's own
shelter — for that is to be taxed not for what the state has given, but for what
nature itself commands every living thing to do, which is to make for itself a home.

Let the particulars be set down, that they may be weighed by plain men:

— It has laid its hand upon the materials of a dwelling before ever they became a
dwelling, and called the gathering of one's own shelter a sale to be taxed.

— It has taxed the beam and the board, the nail and the glass — the very bones of a
home — as though a roof over a family were an indulgence, and not the first necessity
of a people in a cold country.

— It has drawn no honest line between the speculator who builds to sell at a profit
and the father who builds only to live, but has reached into the pocket of the one who
merely shelters with the very same hand that reaches for the one who trades.

— It has found grace and exemption for many a structure raised by other hands and for
other ends, yet has found none — none whatsoever — for the single roof a person raises
over the heads of his own household.

— And it has called this exaction a contribution to the common protection, while in
the whole of the transaction it has protected nothing under heaven but its own
revenue.

Now let me speak more nearly, as one man speaking to his own conscience, and inviting
every other to consult his own. The state is a great machine, and most of us, most of
the time, are content to be its smooth and frictionless parts, and there is no shame in
it; the machine does much good. We pay for the road, and we ought to. We pay for the
school and the bridge and the watch kept through the night, and we are right to. But
there comes at last an exaction of a different kind — one that does not ask me to share
the cost of what we hold in common, but asks me to buy back from the public treasury
the right to do the most natural and most private thing a free creature ever does,
which is to raise itself a home. To a demand of that kind a person's conscience may
quietly answer: no. And he may stand upon that no, and let the machine feel that he is
there.

I counsel no tumult, and I throw no tea into no harbor. I counsel only this: that a
free person remember he was a person before ever he was a subject, and a conscience
before ever he was a taxpayer; that he seek his redress in the open court and by the
lawful and reasoned word, as a free people in a free commonwealth are entitled to do;
and that he refuse, gently and immovably, to let so great a right be carried off in so
small a coin — in the little forgettable sum at the bottom of a thousand receipts,
where tyranny, when it is wise, has always preferred to hide.

And so, upon these public stones, beneath a dome that a thrifty people once raised for
themselves without laying upon their neighbors a single new tax to do it, in the salt
wind of a harbor that has carried into this city both honest cargo and unbending
conviction — we do solemnly publish and declare:

That the right to acquire, to possess, and to protect a home is a natural and
unalienable right, and no mere privilege held at the pleasure of any power;

That a free people may be asked to share the common burden, but may not be made to
purchase, at their own thresholds, the leave to shelter their own;

That where consent is the only just foundation of a tax, an exaction laid upon the
materials of one's own dwelling, against which no representative voice was ever truly
raised in the people's name, wants that foundation;

And that we will seek the redress of this wrong by every lawful means a free
commonwealth affords — patiently, openly, and without ceasing.

We pledge it to one another, and to those who will build their homes long after we have
laid down our tools: that we will not suffer the right to a roof to be quietly bought
away from the people in the small change of a tax; and that we will hold fast, against
any power that would forget it, to this one sufficient truth —

**A home is the last fortress of a free people, and they will not be taxed upon its
threshold without their leave.**
