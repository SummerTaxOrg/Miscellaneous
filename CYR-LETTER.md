**Re: Completing the work you began. A building-materials exemption for the family that builds its own home.**

Dear Senator Cyr:

We write as a coalition of owner-builders, general contractors, and home builders to thank you for a genuine achievement and to ask you to finish it. This June the Commonwealth began exempting from the sales and use tax the building materials that go into qualifying multifamily housing, a measure you authored and advanced and that the Governor signed into law. When you announced it you said there is "no one fix," but that the Senate is "determined to make it faster and cheaper to build the homes Massachusetts needs." We agree with every word, and we write because there is a clear next fix that is the natural companion to the one you just won.

We ask you to file and champion an exemption from the 6.25 percent sales and use tax on the building materials a Massachusetts resident purchases to construct or substantially rehabilitate the home in which that resident will live. It is the same relief you secured for multifamily production this session, extended to the family building its own roof. The lumber, the windows, and the wiring are identical whether they go into an apartment building approved for the new exemption or into the modest year-round house a family is raising for itself. Having decided that these materials may be spared when a developer buys them, the Commonwealth has already conceded the principle and shown that its budget can bear the cost. The only judgment it has not yet made is that the family building the home it will live in deserves what the developer building to sell now receives.

Nowhere does this tax bite harder than on the Cape and the Islands, where building costs run well above the mainland because materials must be ferried and trades must be housed. A year-round family on the Vineyard or in Truro that manages, against long odds, to build or rebuild a home is paying the 6.25 percent levy on some of the most expensive building materials in Massachusetts. You have said the region has become "profoundly unaffordable" for year-round families, and that every year families leave because they cannot afford to stay. This is one of the costs that pushes them out.

We are not asking for a loophole for second homes or for the speculative market you have spent years fighting. The exemption would be confined to a natural person building or substantially rehabilitating a primary residence, and would exclude second homes, investment property, developers, and any construction for resale. Drawn that way it does the opposite of subsidizing the second-home market that drives your region's prices: it lowers the cost of the one kind of building that keeps a year-round family rooted in a community. Its cost to the Commonwealth is bounded by the same logic that bounded yours, and a smaller and more sharply defined one. The legislative path is one you have just traveled, since the multifamily exemption rode a supplemental-budget amendment rather than a standalone bill, and as Senate Chair of the Joint Committee on Housing you are better placed than anyone to carry it.

You told the Commonwealth there is no one fix. You delivered one of them this session, and you were right to. We are asking you to author the next, for the teachers, the firefighters, the nurses, and the tradespeople who are trying to build and keep a home in the place they have given their lives to, and who today are taxed for the effort of sheltering themselves. We would welcome the chance to meet with you or your staff to share what our members see on every job and every receipt.

With respect and appreciation for the work you have already done,
