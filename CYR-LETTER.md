**Re: Completing the work you began. A building-materials exemption for the family that builds its own home.**

Dear Senator Cyr:

We write as a coalition of owner-builders, general contractors, and home builders to thank you for a genuine achievement and to ask you to finish it. This June the Commonwealth began exempting from the sales and use tax the building materials that go into qualifying multifamily housing, a measure you authored and advanced and that the Governor signed into law. When you announced it you said something we have been waiting years to hear a legislator say plainly: that the failure to build housing is harming our residents and employers, that there is "no one fix," and that the Senate is "determined to make it faster and cheaper to build the homes Massachusetts needs." We agree with every word of it. We write because there is a next fix, it is the natural companion to the one you just won, and you are the right person to author it.

**The ask.** We ask you to file and champion an exemption from the 6.25 percent sales and use tax on the building materials a Massachusetts resident purchases to construct or substantially rehabilitate the home in which that resident will live. It is the same relief you secured for multifamily production this session, extended to the family building its own roof. The materials are identical. The lumber, the windows, the wiring, and the delivery are the same whether they go into an apartment building approved for the new exemption or into the modest year-round house a family is raising for itself. Having decided that these materials may be spared when a developer buys them to build housing, the Commonwealth has already conceded the principle and shown that its budget can bear the cost. The only judgment it has not yet made is that the family building the home it will live in deserves what the developer building to sell now receives.

**Why this is the same policy, not a new one.** Your multifamily exemption rests on a simple insight: when the Commonwealth taxes the materials of the housing it is trying to encourage, it raises the very cost it says it wants to bring down, and a tax exemption on those materials is a direct and honest way to make building faster and cheaper. That insight does not stop at the property line of a multifamily project. The 6.25 percent the Commonwealth collects on a family's lumber and fixtures is the same self-defeating tax on the same housing goal, paid by the resident least able to spread it across a development pro forma. The owner-builder pays it himself, in his own name, on every receipt, on top of land, labor, and financing. The family that hires the work out pays it too, and pays more, because under the contractor-as-consumer rule the builder is taxed on the materials and prices them at cost plus a margin, so the household is charged a markup on the tax itself. In each case the Commonwealth is taxing a family for the act of housing itself, which is the precise behavior the rest of your housing agenda is built to reward.

**Why it matters most in the district you represent.** Nowhere in the Commonwealth does this tax bite harder than on the Cape and the Islands. Building costs there run well above the mainland, by most accounts ten to twenty percent higher, because materials must be ferried, trades must be housed, and even the disposal of construction debris carries a premium measured in hundreds of dollars a ton on Nantucket. A year-round family on the Vineyard or in Truro that manages, against long odds, to build or rebuild a primary residence is therefore paying the 6.25 percent levy on some of the most expensive building materials in Massachusetts. You have said that the Cape and Islands have become "profoundly unaffordable" for year-round families, and that every year families leave because they cannot afford to stay. This tax is one of the costs that pushes them out, and relieving it is squarely within the work you have already made your own.

**It can be drawn narrowly, and drawn to your own purposes.** We are not asking for a loophole for second homes or for the speculative market you have spent years fighting. The exemption we propose would be confined to a natural person building or substantially rehabilitating a primary residence, and it would exclude second homes, investment property, developers, and any construction for resale. Drawn that way, it does the opposite of subsidizing the second-home market that drives your region's prices: it lowers the cost of the one kind of building that keeps a year-round family rooted in a community, and it leaves the speculator paying full freight. Its fiscal exposure is bounded by the same logic that bounded yours. The multifamily exemption was accepted at a cost the Commonwealth capped and could absorb; an exemption confined to owner-occupied primary residences is a smaller and more sharply defined universe, and a portion of its cost returns to the Commonwealth as the housing it subsidizes through other programs becomes cheaper to build.

**It need not start from nothing.** The Commonwealth already distinguishes taxable from exempt construction materials as a matter of routine, through the exemption certificates it issues for governmental and charitable building projects, so administering an owner-occupant exemption asks nothing new of the Department of Revenue's machinery. The legislative path is one you have just traveled. The multifamily exemption rode a supplemental-budget amendment rather than a standalone bill, and the same vehicle is available here. As Senate Chair of the Joint Committee on Housing you are better placed than any member of the Legislature to see that this relief reaches the families who need it, and to frame it, correctly, as the completion of the seasonal-communities toolkit you have already built for the year-round households of the Cape and Islands.

You told the Commonwealth there is no one fix. You delivered one of them this session, and you were right to. We are asking you to author the next one, for the teachers, the firefighters, the nurses, the tradespeople, and the working families who are trying to build and keep a home in the place they have given their lives to, and who today are taxed for the effort of sheltering themselves. We would welcome the chance to meet with you or your staff, to share what our members see on every job and every receipt, and to help in any way that is useful as you consider it.

With respect and appreciation for the work you have already done,
