## STATEMENT OF THE INTEREST OF THE AMICI CURIAE

The amici are the men and women who build the Commonwealth's homes.

**The Bay State Council of General Contractors & Home Builders** is a nonprofit association whose members are licensed general contractors, home-building firms, and the tradespeople they employ, engaged across the Commonwealth in the construction of single-family and multifamily dwellings. The undersigned **master builders** are individual contractors who have, between them, framed, roofed, wired, and finished [thousands of] Massachusetts homes. The amici join this brief not as a matter of abstract civic interest but because the question before the Court reaches into the daily practice of their trade and the household budgets of every family for whom they build.

The amici's interest is direct and concrete. Under the Commissioner's longstanding **contractor-as-consumer** rule, a contractor who incorporates building materials into real property is treated as the *consumer* of those materials and is the taxpayer who pays the sales or use tax upon them. *See* DOR Directive 02-16; Letter Ruling 80-44; 830 CMR 64H.1.1. The amici are therefore not bystanders to the tax the Plaintiff-Appellant challenges. They *pay* it — on the lumber, the framing, the windows, the wire, and the fixtures of every house they raise — and they watch it pass, dollar for dollar, into the price a Massachusetts family must pay for a roof. The owner-builder who buys those same materials at the same register bears the identical charge in his own name. The constitutional principle that governs the one governs the other, and the amici have lived its practical weight on every job site in the Commonwealth.

The amici submit this brief because they are uniquely positioned to show the Court what the tax actually falls upon — not luxury, not commerce for its own sake, but shelter itself, taken apart and taxed piece by piece — and because the resolution of this question will affect the cost of housing for the families they serve and the livelihoods of those who build it. The amici respectfully hope their practical vantage will aid the Court's consideration of the issue.

## ISSUE ADDRESSED BY THE AMICI

Whether the imposition of the Massachusetts sales and use tax, G.L. c. 64H and c. 64I, upon building materials and supplies purchased to construct an individual's own single-family residence offends the guarantees of the Massachusetts Declaration of Rights that secure the acquisition and protection of property (art. 1, as amended by art. 106) and that forbid the laying of any tax without the consent of the people or their representatives (arts. 10, 23), as reinforced by the due-process guarantee (art. 12) and the requirement that an excise be reasonable (Pt. 2, c. 1, § 1, art. 4).

---

## SUMMARY OF ARGUMENT

1. The principle that a free people are taxed only by their own consent, and that the protection of the home is the very end for which government is instituted, is not a modern gloss laid upon the Declaration of Rights. It is that charter's original and deliberate meaning, written in 1780 by the hand of John Adams and forged in the decade of grievance that preceded it. Articles 1, 10, and 23 are not loose aspirations; they are precise reservations of right, and they speak directly to a charge laid upon the materials of a family's home. *(Argument I.)*

2. As applied to the materials of a personal residence, the tax exceeds the boundary the property guarantees set upon the revenue power. The home is the most concrete exercise of the Article 1 right to acquire, possess, and protect property; a tax upon its very bones is a charge upon the exercise of that right, and one that does not bear a real and substantial relation to a legitimate public end when the same Commonwealth exempts other construction. *(Argument II.)*

3. The amici's trade supplies what argument alone cannot. Under the contractor-as-consumer rule the builders before this Court are themselves the statutory taxpayers, and they can attest that the tax falls upon shelter itself and is borne, in the end, by every family that builds. The Legislature's own 2026 exemption for multifamily construction materials demonstrates that the line drawn against the single-family home is one of administrative convenience, not constitutional principle. *(Argument III.)*

4. The question is one of pure law, of recurring and broad public significance — it touches every home built in the Commonwealth — and its resolution would terminate the controversy. This Court should reach it and declare the tax invalid as applied to the materials of an owner-builder's personal residence. *(Argument IV.)*

## ARGUMENT

### I. The Commonwealth's founding charter — drafted to secure the home and to forbid taxation without consent — speaks directly to this exaction.

#### A. The Declaration of Rights is a deliberate, knowable text, and its meaning is illuminated by the hand that wrote it and the grievances it answered.

This Court does not read the Declaration of Rights as a set of pious generalities. It reads it as law — and, where its words are old, as law whose meaning is illuminated by the circumstances of its making. The Constitution of the Commonwealth of Massachusetts, ratified in 1780 and the oldest functioning written constitution in the world, was drafted in its essential parts by John Adams. The property and consent guarantees the Plaintiff-Appellant invokes are his words.

They were not written in tranquility. They were written by men who had spent the prior fifteen years contending, in the town meetings of this Commonwealth, against a distant power that taxed them without their consent and that treated the threshold of a private home as no barrier to the revenue officer. The conviction that emerged from that decade — that a people consent to taxation through their own representatives and through no one else, and that the home is the citadel the law exists to protect — was not rhetoric to the framers. It was the hard-won premise of the government they were building. When Adams set down, in Article 1, the natural right of "acquiring, possessing, and protecting property," and in Article 23 the command that "no subsidy, charge, tax, impost, or duties, ought to be established, fixed, laid, or levied, under any pretext whatsoever, without the consent of the people, or their representatives in the legislature," he was reducing to fundamental law the very principle for which a Commonwealth had just been pledged.

The amici do not ask the Court to be moved by sentiment. They ask the Court to give these words the weight their authors gave them. A guarantee written to protect the home against an unconsented exaction does not lose its force because the exaction now arrives quietly, at a lumber-yard register, in the small coin of a receipt, rather than by proclamation from across an ocean. The dignity of the right is measured by its object — the shelter of a family — and not by the size of any single charge laid against it.

#### B. Articles 1, 10, and 23 together reserve to the people both the protection of the home and the consent condition on every tax.

Read together, as they were meant to be, four provisions of Part the First operate as a single guarantee. Article 1 establishes the acquisition and protection of property as a natural and enumerated right. Article 10 secures to each individual the "enjoyment of his life, liberty and property, according to standing laws," houses the principle that property may not be "applied to public uses, without his own consent, or that of the representative body of the people," and promises "reasonable compensation" where property is appropriated to public use. Article 12 guarantees that no subject be deprived of property but by "the law of the land." Article 23 states the taxation-consent principle in its plainest form.

The amici are mindful — and candid with the Court — that these same clauses *authorize* taxation by legislative consent, and oblige each individual to "contribute his share to the expense of [the common] protection." The principle is not that taxation offends the property right; plainly it does not. The principle, rather, is that these clauses set a *boundary*: they mark the line beyond which an exaction ceases to be a shared contribution to the common protection and becomes instead a charge laid upon the very thing the charter exists to protect. The question in this case is whether a tax upon the materials of a family's own home falls on the wrong side of that line. The amici submit that, as applied, it does.

### II. As applied to the materials of a family's own home, the tax exceeds the boundary the property guarantees set upon the revenue power.

#### A. The home is the core application of the Article 1 property right, and a tax on its very materials charges the exercise of that right.

The right to "acquire, possess, and protect property" is nowhere more concretely exercised than in the building and owning of a home. A dwelling a family builds for itself is not merchandise held for trade; it is the most personal property a person acquires in a lifetime, and the protection of it is among the oldest objects of the law. A tax imposed on the *inputs* to that act — the timber before it is a wall, the nail, the beam, the pane of glass — is not an incidental regulation of commerce. It is a charge upon shelter itself, disassembled into its parts and taxed at each one. The closer a tax draws to the core of an enumerated right, the more it demands a justification answering to that right.

#### B. The exaction does not bear a real and substantial relation to a legitimate public end as applied to personal shelter.

This Court reviews economic and property measures to ensure they "bear[] a real and substantial relation to the public health, safety, morals, or some other phase of the general welfare." *Goodridge* v. *Department of Pub. Health*, 440 Mass. 309, 330 (2003), quoting *Coffee-Rich, Inc.* v. *Commissioner of Pub. Health*, 348 Mass. 414, 422 (1965). The amici do not quarrel with the Commonwealth's power to raise revenue, nor with the general validity of the sales and use tax. They contend only that, *as applied to the materials of a personal residence* — and in a Commonwealth that has chosen to exempt the materials of other construction (Argument III.B) — the charge wants the real and substantial relation the standard requires. Revenue convenience is not, by itself, that relation; if it were, the standard would be no standard at all. A levy that in substance reaches the materials of one's own shelter, while sparing comparable construction, is precisely the kind of exaction the property guarantees were written to confine.

#### C. The consent the property clauses require was given to the common burden — not to a charge upon shelter itself.

The Commonwealth will answer, fairly, that the Legislature enacted this tax and that Article 23's consent condition is therefore met. The amici meet the point directly. Consent to the general burden of the State — to the cost of the road, the school, the court, and the common defense — is not, and was never understood to be, a standing consent to tax the citizen for the act of sheltering his own family. The framers who wrote the consent condition drew exactly this distinction between the common charge a free people undertake together and the particular exaction laid upon the necessaries of private life. The tax here is sustained, if at all, only by treating a family's home as ordinary merchandise. The property guarantees forbid that equation.

### III. The amici's vantage: this is a tax on the bones of a house, borne by every family that builds, and the Legislature's own 2026 exemption exposes the line drawn against the family home as one of convenience.

#### A. Under the contractor-as-consumer rule, the builders before this Court are themselves the taxpayers, and they know the tax falls on shelter.

The amici speak from inside the transaction the Court is asked to examine. Under the Commissioner's settled rule, a contractor who incorporates materials into real property is the *consumer* of those materials and pays the sales or use tax upon them. *See* DOR Directive 02-16; Letter Ruling 80-44; 830 CMR 64H.1.1. The amici therefore pay this tax on every house they build, and it passes into the price a family pays for a home. The owner-builder who buys the same materials bears the same charge in his own name. Whether the buyer is the family's contractor or the family itself, the tax falls in the same place: upon the materials of a dwelling. The amici can attest, from the ledger of every project, that what is taxed here is not luxury or speculation but the structural necessities of human shelter.

#### B. The 2026 multifamily exemption shows the line excluding the single-family home is arbitrary, not principled.

In 2026 the Legislature enacted a sales-tax exemption, capped at approximately $35 million annually, for building materials and supplies consumed in approved multifamily housing projects. St. 2026, c. [___]. The amici build under that exemption and welcome it. But its existence answers any suggestion that the Commonwealth *cannot* relieve construction materials of this tax, or that doing so would derange the revenue: the Legislature has done precisely that for one category of housing. The materials of a family's single-family home are chemically and structurally identical to the materials of an exempt multifamily project; the same lumber, the same wire, the same glass. The line that taxes the one and exempts the other is a line of administrative selection, not of constitutional principle. That the line exists at all confirms that the burden on the owner-builder is a legislative choice subject to the property guarantees — and sharpens, rather than answers, the constitutional infirmity the Plaintiff-Appellant identifies.

### IV. The question is one of pure law and broad public significance; this Court should reach it and resolve it.

The amici urge the Court to decide the question presented and not to turn it aside on prudential grounds. The issue is one of pure law: it does not turn on any disputed fact about particular receipts, but on the constitutional validity of a category of taxation. It is of broad and recurring public significance: it touches every home built in the Commonwealth and every family that undertakes to build one, at a moment when the cost and supply of housing are matters of acknowledged statewide concern. And its resolution would terminate the controversy rather than leave it to recur, transaction by transaction, in the abatement process. *See* G.L. c. 231A, §§ 1, 2; *cf. ACE Prop. & Cas. Ins. Co.* v. *Commissioner of Revenue*, 437 Mass. 241 (2002); *DiStefano* v. *Commissioner of Revenue*, 394 Mass. 315 (1985). These are the circumstances in which a declaration of right is most warranted, and in which this Court's guidance is most needed.

---

## CONCLUSION

The men and women who build the Commonwealth's homes do not come to this Court to ask that they be relieved of their fair share of the common burden. They come to ask that an old and deliberate promise be kept. The charter of this Commonwealth was written to secure to every family the right to acquire and protect a home, and to ensure that no charge be laid upon the people save by their own consent — and it was written by those who had learned, at no small cost, what it means to be taxed at one's own threshold without leave. A tax upon the very materials of a family's home, sustained only by treating that home as ordinary merchandise while the Commonwealth exempts the materials of other construction, cannot be reconciled with that promise.

For the foregoing reasons, the amici curiae respectfully urge this Court to reverse the judgment below and to declare that the imposition of the Massachusetts sales and use tax upon building materials purchased to construct an individual owner-builder's single-family residence, as applied, violates the guarantees of Articles 1, 10, 12, and 23 of the Massachusetts Declaration of Rights.
