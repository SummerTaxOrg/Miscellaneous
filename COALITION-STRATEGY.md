# Coalition & Sponsor Map: The Owner-Occupied Materials Exemption

**Purpose.** Build the legislative and outside-organization coalition for a bill exempting the
6.25% sales/use tax on building materials a person buys to construct or substantially
rehabilitate their **owner-occupied primary residence** — the homeowner complement to the
multifamily materials exemption (**S.3041**) the Legislature enacted in June 2026. This map
identifies who to recruit, why each is reachable, and the frame that moves them, so the bill
has a constituency before it has a docket number.

**Compiled:** June 20, 2026. Sourcing and confidence conventions are in the file header.

---

## 1. The strategic frame (read first)

Three facts shape every approach below:

1. **The mechanism is already conceded.** The Legislature just exempted construction materials
   from the sales tax for multifamily housing (S.3041, ~$35M/yr cap, signed June 2026). Our
   bill is not a novel idea the body must be talked into; it is the **same tool extended to the
   family building its own home.** This neutralizes the "untested / can't afford it" objection.
   Lead with "you already did this for developers; do it for homeowners." [ON RECORD: the
   multifamily exemption] (https://www.bostonglobe.com/2026/04/02/business/construction-sales-tax/ ;
   https://malegislature.gov/PressRoom/Detail?pressReleaseId=351)
2. **It is simultaneously a tax cut and a housing measure.** That duality is the source of its
   bipartisan reach: it speaks to Democrats as housing affordability and to Republicans as tax
   relief. Few housing bills can be pitched honestly to both caucuses; this one can.
3. **Its central vulnerability is the missing affordability screen.** S.3041 is targeted (15%
   affordable units, or sub-120%-AMI communities). An owner-occupant version that benefits all
   primary-residence builders — including high earners — will draw scrutiny from
   affordability-first members and groups (Eldridge, CHAPA, possibly Spilka). The design answer
   is to **confine it to primary residences (no second homes, no resale)** and to be ready to
   discuss a **price cap or means element** if needed. Anticipate this in every progressive
   conversation. [INFERENCE, strongly supported] (see §5)

### Committee gatekeepers (the bill's actual path: Revenue, then Ways & Means)

A tax-exemption bill is referred to the **Joint Committee on Revenue (J26)**, then **Ways and
Means**, before the floor. The Housing Committee is friendly (Cyr chairs it), but Revenue is the
real gate.

- **Sen. James (Jamie) Eldridge** (D) — **Senate Revenue Chair, and the principal obstacle.**
  On record skeptical of tax cuts ("I don't see cutting taxes as the way to get there").
  [ON RECORD] (https://ballotpedia.org/James_Eldridge). **Manage, do not lead with him:** frame
  as housing production and base the ask on the precedent the Senate already set; bring a tight
  fiscal number; consider routing via a budget rider (as S.3041 traveled) to limit his
  gatekeeping leverage.
- **Rep. Adrian Madaro** (D) — **House Revenue Chair**; actively handles sales-tax-exemption
  bills. A pragmatic, reachable chair. (https://eastietimes.com/2025/03/12/madaro-appointed-house-chair-of-the-joint-committee-on-revenue/)
- **Friendly Revenue members:** Sen. **Ryan Fattman** (R, ranking) and Rep. **Marc Lombardo**
  (R, ranking) / Rep. **Nicholas Boldyga** (R) — reliable votes for a tax cut on the panel.

## 3. Outside-organization coalition — tiered

No organization has an on-record position on *this specific* (unfiled) bill; every stance below
is **[INFERENCE]** from a documented record. Entry points (lobbyists/leaders) are named where
found.

### Tier 1 — Natural leads (direct beneficiaries, clean fit)

- **Home Builders & Remodelers Association of MA (HBRAMA)** — *the anchor.* 2,000+ builders and
  remodelers; its single-family/owner-occupied base is precisely the constituency S.3041 left
  out. Affordability/"American Dream of homeownership" record; sat on the housing-production
  commission (past Pres. **Jeffrey Brem**). **Entry point: lobbyist Benjamin Fierro III** (Lynch &
  Fierro). Likely lead coalition partner. (https://hbrama.com/ ; http://www.lynchfierro.com/about-us/benjamin-fierro-iii/)
- **Massachusetts Association of Realtors (MAR)** — "can't solve a supply problem by making
  housing more expensive"; backs first-time-homebuyer tax-advantaged accounts; 400+ member
  "REALTOR Day on Beacon Hill." Cleanest mission fit after HBRAMA. (https://www.marealtor.com/legislative-priorities/)
- **MA Retail Lumber Dealers Association (MRLDA)** — sells the taxed materials; explicit
  affordability frame (opposed mandates that "add up to $20,000 to the cost of a new home").
  **Entry point: lobbyist Patrick Huntington** (Mass Bay Associates). Caveat: their support may
  hinge on the exemption being **administratively clean at the point of sale.** (https://nrla.org/state-locals/mrlda/)
- **Massachusetts Fiscal Alliance** — reflexively pro-tax-relief; brings grassroots/anti-tax
  mobilization and legislator scorecards. Strong brand fit. (https://massfiscal.org/about/)
- **Abundant Housing MA (YIMBY)** — strongest *ideological* fit (cutting input costs to boost
  supply is core abundance logic). **Entry point: ED Jesse Kanson-Benanav.** Caveat: usually uses
  regulatory not tax tools, and may want the bill broadened beyond owner-occupied. (https://www.abundanthousingma.org/2025-2026-priorities/)

### Tier 2 — Supportive, secondary

- **Greater Boston Real Estate Board (GBREB)** — residential (GBAR) wing aligns; commercial/rental
  divisions are indifferent to an owner-occupied-only bill. Real but secondary support.
- **NAIOP Massachusetts** — **on record endorsing the multifamily S.3041** (CEO Tamara Small,
  ~2.5% cost cut). Its members build commercial/multifamily, so our bill is orthogonal to them;
  **frame ours as a complement, not a competitor for the same fiscal cap**, to keep NAIOP
  neutral-to-supportive. (https://www.bostonglobe.com/2026/04/02/business/construction-sales-tax/)
- **Pioneer Institute** — free-market think tank; value is **intellectual credibility** (research,
  op-eds, testimony) on the tax-burden and housing-cost case, not lobbying.
- **Housing Assistance Corporation (HAC, Cape Cod)** — a winnable regional supporter via its
  "missing middle" framing and the **"Housing to Protect Cape Cod"** coalition. **Entry point:
  CEO Alisa Magnotta.**

### Manage carefully

- **Massachusetts Taxpayers Foundation (MTF)** — *not* an anti-tax group; a fiscal-discipline
  analyst whose authoritative cost estimate could **help or hurt.** Most likely to flag base
  erosion and question a non-means-tested exemption. **Engage early with a tight fiscal number**
  so their analysis lands favorably. (https://www.masstaxpayers.org/)
- **CHAPA** (affordable-housing umbrella) — prefers income-targeted tools; a non-targeted
  owner-occupant exemption runs against its equity framing. Unlikely to actively oppose; expect a
  **means-test/price-cap ask.** Frame in production and affordability terms. (https://chapa.org/)

### The building trades — conditional, manage expectations

The Massachusetts Building Trades Unions, **Laborers (LIUNA)**, and **Carpenters (NASRCC)** fund
housing champions and back production *built with union labor and wage standards.* For an
**owner-occupied / owner-builder** bill — largely non-union residential work — they are
**neutral-to-skeptical**, the Carpenters most of all (their agenda treats non-union residential
construction as a locus of wage theft and misclassification). [INFERENCE; no on-record position
found.] **Lever, if their support is sought:** attach responsible-contractor / anti-misclassification
language to the *hire-a-contractor* portion of the bill. Do not assume they are allies merely
because they fund Cyr; the multifamily bill they could embrace, this one is harder for them.
(https://www.nasrcc.org/new-study-examines-wage-theft-in-ma-construction/)

## 5. The two design questions the coalition will force

These are not rhetorical; the answers determine who signs on. Decide them before filing.

1. **Affordability screen — yes or no?** A clean, all-primary-residence exemption maximizes the
   tax-cut/GOP/homebuilder coalition but draws fire from Eldridge, CHAPA, MTF, and possibly
   Spilka. A **price cap** (e.g., exemption only up to a stated home-value or materials-cost
   ceiling) or a **first-/primary-home means element** broadens the progressive coalition at the
   cost of some Republican purity and added complexity. **Recommendation: confine tightly to
   primary residences and prepare a price-cap fallback** to offer if leadership demands targeting.
2. **Labor standards — silent or conditioned?** Silence keeps the bill clean and keeps Republicans
   and owner-builders happy; adding responsible-contractor language to the hire-a-contractor
   portion could win or neutralize the trades but complicates the bill and may cost GOP votes.
   **Recommendation: file clean; hold responsible-contractor language in reserve** as a
   negotiating chip if the trades organize against it.

## 6. Recommended outreach sequence

1. **Lock the champion.** Cyr first (already in motion via CYR-LETTER.md); secure his willingness
   to lead or co-lead.
2. **Get the leadership read.** Through Cyr, test Spilka's appetite and learn whether she requires
   an affordability screen — this determines bill design before anything is public.
3. **Add the bipartisan face.** Recruit Tarr (R) as Senate co-lead and Markey (D, Housing + H.3180)
   as the homeownership bridge; line up Haggerty as House co-lead.
4. **Pre-wire Revenue.** Brief Madaro (House Revenue) and prepare for Eldridge (Senate Revenue);
   commission the fiscal estimate now so MTF and Eldridge meet a number, not a vacuum.
5. **Stand up the outside coalition.** Lead with HBRAMA (Fierro), MAR, MRLDA (Huntington),
   Abundant Housing (Kanson-Benanav), MassFiscal; add HAC and the Cape coalition; engage MTF early
   and CHAPA carefully.
6. **Choose the vehicle.** Standalone bill (Revenue → Ways & Means → floor) versus a
   **supplemental-budget rider** (the route S.3041 actually traveled, which limits a hostile
   Revenue chair's leverage). The rider precedent is strong and worth serious consideration.
