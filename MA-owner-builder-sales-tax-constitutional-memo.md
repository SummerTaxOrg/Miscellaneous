## 1. TAX MECHANICS

### A. The "contractor-as-consumer" rule (baseline)

Massachusetts imposes a 6.25% sales tax on retail sales of tangible personal property (G.L. c. 64H, § 2) and a complementary use tax at the same rate on the storage, use, or other consumption of such property in the Commonwealth (G.L. c. 64I, § 2). The use tax backstops the sales tax for untaxed/out-of-state purchases, with a credit for sales tax lawfully paid to another State (TIR 03-1).

For construction, the controlling administrative rule treats a **contractor as the consumer** of the building materials it incorporates into real property. Because the contractor is deemed to "consume" the materials in performing a real-property contract (rather than reselling tangible personal property to the owner), the *contractor* pays sales/use tax on its **cost of materials**, and the subsequent transfer of the finished improvement to the owner is not itself a taxable retail sale. See:
- **DOR Directive 02-16** (construing "building materials and supplies" and "construction vehicles, equipment and machinery" under G.L. c. 64H, § 6(f));
- **Letter Ruling 80-44** ("Materials Purchased by Construction Contractor" — materials are taxable to the contractor as consumer);
- **830 CMR 64H.1.1** (Service Enterprises) (treating contractors performing real-property work as consumers of the property they install, and prescribing certificate practice).

*Binding vs. persuasive:* The statutes (c. 64H/64I) are binding. The regulation (830 CMR 64H.1.1) has the force of law if within the Commissioner's authority. Directives and Letter Rulings are the Commissioner's interpretive positions — persuasive and binding on DOR, but not on a court.

### B. How the analysis shifts for an OWNER-BUILDER

When the **owner-builder buys materials directly**, the analysis collapses to the simplest case under c. 64H/64I, and the contractor-as-consumer construct is not even needed:

- **At the register (sales tax).** The owner-builder is the retail purchaser/consumer of tangible personal property. The vendor collects 6.25% sales tax at the point of sale (G.L. c. 64H, §§ 2, 3). The owner-builder bears and pays the tax directly.
- **Untaxed/out-of-state (use tax).** For materials bought out of state or otherwise without Massachusetts tax, the owner-builder owes **use tax** at 6.25% (G.L. c. 64I, § 2) and is the statutory taxpayer obligated to **self-assess and remit** it.

The practical upshot: an owner-builder is, unambiguously, *the person on whom the tax is imposed* — both as the consumer paying sales tax and as the taxpayer self-assessing use tax. This is the crux of the cleaner standing posture discussed in Part 3.

### C. Existing exemptions — none reaches the individual owner-builder

- **G.L. c. 64H, § 6(f) / Form ST-5C (exempt-entity projects).** Exempts building materials and supplies consumed in construction of a building or structure owned by, or held in trust for, a governmental body or a § 501(c)(3) organization, where used exclusively for the exempt purpose. A contractor on such a project tenders Form **ST-5C** to buy materials tax-free. *This is an exempt-owner/exempt-use exemption; a private individual building a personal residence does not qualify.*
- **G.L. c. 64H, § 6(r)/(s) / Form ST-12 (manufacturing, R&D, etc.).** "Exempt use" exemptions for materials/machinery consumed in manufacturing, processing, R&D, etc. *Plainly inapplicable to residential homebuilding.*
- **No "owner-builder of a single-family home" exemption exists** anywhere in c. 64H, § 6. The statute's exemptions are owner-status or use-category driven; the personal homeowner falls into none of them.

**Conclusion (Part 1.C):** As of June 2026, no statutory or regulatory exemption shelters an individual owner-builder's purchases of materials for a single-family home.

### D. The 2026 Senate measure (~$35M construction-materials exemption)

In its FY2026 work (Senate budget / consolidated "Fair Share" supplemental package), the Senate advanced — and reporting indicates the Governor signed — a **new sales-tax exemption for building materials and supplies, capped at ~$35 million annually**, targeted at **multifamily housing production**. Key features as reported:
- Eligibility limited to **approved multifamily projects**, prioritized by the Executive Office of Housing and Livable Communities (EOHLC);
- Targeting criteria such as **≥15% affordable units** and/or location in communities below a defined income threshold;
- Projected to support tens of thousands of housing units over ~10 years.

**Critical point for the client:** The measure is a *capped, discretionary, project-based* program for *multifamily/affordable* development administered through EOHLC. By its terms it does **not** reach an **individual owner-builder constructing a single-family home for personal occupancy**. If anything, the new exemption *sharpens an equal-protection-flavored framing* (the Legislature now exempts some construction materials but not the owner-builder's), while simultaneously *cutting against* a facial challenge (it demonstrates the Legislature making precisely the line-drawing/classification choices that courts defer to in the excise context — see Part 4).

> **Verify before relying:** confirm the enacted bill's chapter/section, effective date, and final eligibility text. Treat the "signed into law" status as reported-but-unconfirmed pending a pull of the session law.

## 2. PROCEDURAL PATHWAY (core question)

### A. Facial/categorical challenge vs. as-applied dispute

- A **facial/categorical** challenge attacks the *statute itself* — the contention that the Commonwealth may not constitutionally tax this category (home-construction materials of an owner-builder) at all. Relief sought is a **declaration of invalidity** and prospective injunction, not a refund tied to specific receipts. The natural vehicle is a **declaratory judgment** under **G.L. c. 231A**.
- An **as-applied** dispute contests the tax on *particular purchases/assessments* (amount, taxability of specific items, credit for out-of-state tax). The exclusive statutory channel is **abatement → Appellate Tax Board (ATB) → appellate review**.

These two tracks have different forums, defendants, deadlines, and — critically — different exhaustion consequences. The pleading must commit to one theory of relief and frame the record accordingly (Part 2.D).

### B. The facial vehicle: G.L. c. 231A declaratory judgment

**Statutory architecture:**
- **§ 1 (jurisdiction & actual controversy).** The Supreme Judicial Court, Superior Court, Land Court, and Probate Courts may make binding declarations of right "in any case in which an *actual controversy* has arisen." The controversy must be real and definite, not hypothetical.
- **§ 2 (applicable controversies).** Expressly reaches challenges to the "validity" of a statute, ordinance, or administrative regulation — i.e., constitutional attacks on a tax statute are within the chapter's contemplated subject matter.
- **§ 3 (discretionary refusal).** The court "may refuse to render a declaratory judgment ... where such judgment ... would not terminate the uncertainty or controversy." This is the textual seat of the **exhaustion/prudential-abstention** doctrine in tax cases (Part 2.D).
- **§ 8 (necessary parties; AG notice).** All persons with an interest that would be affected must be joined; and **where a statute, ordinance, or franchise is "alleged to be unconstitutional, the attorney general shall ... be entitled to be heard."** Serve the Attorney General.
- **Mass. R. Civ. P. 57.** Confirms that the existence of another adequate remedy does not, by itself, bar declaratory relief, but the *procedure* tracks ordinary civil actions; speedy hearing may be ordered.

**Proper court.** Concurrent original jurisdiction lies in the **Superior Court** (the ordinary choice) and the **SJC** (a single justice of the SJC for Suffolk County can entertain such actions, and the full SJC may take novel constitutional tax questions of statewide importance). Filing in Superior Court preserves an appeal of right; an SJC single-justice filing positions the matter for direct SJC attention but is discretionary.

**Proper defendant.** The **Commissioner of Revenue** (the official charged with administering and collecting the tax) is the indispensable defendant. Naming the **Commonwealth** and serving the **Attorney General** (mandatory under § 8 for a constitutional attack) is prudent.

### C. The as-applied vehicle: abatement → ATB → appellate review

1. **Application for abatement to the Commissioner — G.L. c. 62C, § 37.** "Any person aggrieved by the assessment of a tax" may apply, by the later of: 3 years from return filing, 2 years from assessment, or **1 year from payment**. For a self-assessed use tax, the owner-builder files the return, pays, then applies for abatement.
2. **Appeal to the Appellate Tax Board — G.L. c. 62C, § 39.** If the Commissioner denies (or is deemed to deny) the application, the taxpayer petitions the ATB **within 60 days of the notice of denial** (or within 6 months of a deemed denial). The ATB is a quasi-judicial agency with tax expertise.
3. **Pay-to-appeal.** For self-assessed/state taxes the practical rule is **pay first**: the tax must generally be paid as a predicate to abatement and ATB jurisdiction (c. 62C, § 37 keys the limitations period to payment; the ATB's jurisdiction over a c. 62C, § 39 appeal presupposes a properly paid-and-denied abatement). Collection is stayed while a *timely* abatement/appeal is pending, but the jurisdictional predicate for the ATB is the paid-and-denied abatement.
4. **Appellate review — G.L. c. 58A, § 13.** From a final ATB decision, "an appeal as to matters of law may be taken to the **Appeals Court**" (and thence to the SJC by further appellate review). Section 13 makes this "the exclusive method of reviewing any action of the board." Note that small-claims-procedure ATB decisions (c. 58A, § 7B) are **not** judicially reviewable — so do **not** elect small claims if appellate review of a legal question is the goal.

### D. The EXHAUSTION question — when § 3 forces the claim into the ATB track, and how to plead around it

**The default rule is adverse.** Massachusetts courts treat the abatement/ATB process as the **preferred and ordinarily exclusive** route for tax disputes, and will decline declaratory relief under c. 231A, § 3 where an adequate administrative remedy exists:
- **DiStefano v. Commissioner of Revenue, 394 Mass. 315, 321 (1985)** — "the Appellate Tax Board is the preferred forum for resolving questions of this type because of its special expertise in tax matters."
- **ACE Property & Casualty Ins. Co. v. Commissioner of Revenue, 437 Mass. 241, 243–244 (2002)** — declaratory relief in tax matters is confined to a "narrow set of circumstances" that excuse the preliminary exhaustion of administrative remedies.
- **DeMoranville v. Commissioner of Revenue, 457 Mass. 30, 34 (2010)** — reaffirming the exhaustion preference and its limits.

**The recognized exception** — the lane the client must occupy — applies where the question is:
1. **a pure question of law** (no material factual dispute about the transactions),
2. **of broad/recurrent public significance**, and
3. one whose resolution would **terminate the controversy** (231A, § 1/§ 3).

**Pleading framework to keep a genuine facial challenge in Superior Court/SJC:**
- **Frame the relief as categorical, not transactional.** Seek a declaration that c. 64H/64I, *as applied to the category of owner-builder home-construction materials*, violates a specified constitutional clause — not a refund of itemized receipts. A refund prayer invites a § 3 dismissal into abatement.
- **Stipulate away the facts.** Plead that there is *no dispute* the client paid the tax / owes the use tax; the only question is the statute's constitutional validity. This removes any "ATB fact-finding expertise" rationale.
- **Plead the public-significance and recurrence elements expressly** (the tax recurs on every owner-builder; the question is one of general statewide importance — reinforced, ironically, by the Legislature's 2026 attention to construction-materials taxation).
- **Name the Commissioner and serve the Attorney General** (§ 8), and pray that the declaration would "terminate the controversy."
- **Belt-and-suspenders:** Because the exception is narrow and its application uncertain, *preserve the as-applied track in parallel* — file a protective abatement under c. 62C, § 37 within the limitations window so the claim is not time-barred if the c. 231A action is dismissed under § 3. The two are not mutually exclusive; a court may even stay one pending the other.

> **Unsettled:** Whether a court will treat *this* challenge as fitting the ACE Property "narrow set of circumstances" is genuinely uncertain and fact/framing dependent. The stronger the facial/pure-law framing and the public-significance showing, the better the odds of staying in Superior Court/SJC; any reliance on disputed transaction facts pushes the case into the ATB.

## 3. STANDING

### A. The elements — federal frame and the Massachusetts "aggrieved person" standard

Massachusetts has no Article III "case or controversy" clause, but its standing doctrine is functionally similar and is operationalized through (i) c. 231A's **"actual controversy"** requirement and (ii) the recurring statutory phrase **"person aggrieved."** The three familiar elements map cleanly:
- **Injury-in-fact** — a concrete, particularized, non-speculative harm. Massachusetts requires a "real and specific" individual grievance and rejects abstract/generalized complaints. See **Doe v. Governor, 381 Mass. 702 (1980)** (complaint defective for failing to allege injury in fact to a specific, aggrieved individual); **Slama v. Attorney General, 384 Mass. 620 (1981)** (standing turns on a concrete stake; a party without a particularized interest lacks standing).
- **Causation/traceability** — the injury must flow from the challenged tax statute.
- **Redressability** — a declaration of invalidity (and refund/cessation) would remedy the injury.

For the **administrative track**, the statutory standing key is **"any person aggrieved by the assessment of a tax"** (G.L. c. 62C, § 37). The taxpayer who paid (or self-assessed) the tax is the paradigmatic aggrieved person.

### B. Why the OWNER-BUILDER has cleaner standing than a homeowner who hires a contractor

This is the single most important structural point:
- **Owner-builder (direct taxpayer).** The owner-builder *is* the consumer who pays sales tax at the register and *is* the statutory taxpayer who self-assesses and remits use tax. The injury is direct, concrete, out-of-pocket, and traceable to the statute. The owner-builder is squarely a "person aggrieved" under c. 62C, § 37, and has an "actual controversy" under c. 231A, § 1.
- **Homeowner who hires a contractor (pass-through).** Under the contractor-as-consumer rule (Part 1.A), the **contractor**, not the homeowner, is the taxpayer; the homeowner bears the tax only *economically*, embedded in the contract price. That homeowner is **not** the person on whom the tax is imposed and is generally **not** a "person aggrieved" entitled to seek abatement — the classic **pass-through/indirect-payer standing bar**. Only the contractor (the statutory taxpayer) could pursue abatement, and the contractor typically lacks incentive to litigate the homeowner's constitutional theory.

The owner-builder thus *cures the standing defect* that would sink a hire-a-contractor homeowner. The recommended posture — owner-builder **self-assessing use tax** and then challenging — produces the cleanest possible plaintiff: a named taxpayer, a quantified payment, a direct statutory obligation.

### C. Supporting authority and the general-grievance limit

- **Taxpayer/aggrieved standing:** Doe v. Governor and Slama (supra) anchor the "real and specific injury" requirement; c. 62C, § 37's "person aggrieved" supplies the statutory hook for the direct payer.
- **General-grievance limit:** A plaintiff complaining only as a taxpayer-qua-citizen about how public money is taxed/spent, without a particularized injury, generally lacks standing (the Doe v. Governor "specific grievance" rule). The owner-builder avoids this by pleading their *own* tax payments, not a generalized objection to the sales tax as a citizen.

## 4. CONSTITUTIONAL HOOK

The decisive structural fact governs every theory: **the Massachusetts sales/use tax is an *excise* on commodities, not a tax on property.**

> **Mass. Const. Part 2, c. 1, § 1, Art. 4** empowers the General Court (i) "to impose and levy *proportional and reasonable* assessments, rates, and taxes, upon all the inhabitants ... and estates ... within the ... Commonwealth; and *also* (ii) to impose and levy *reasonable* duties and excises upon any produce, goods, wares, merchandise, and commodities ... brought into, produced, manufactured, or being within the same."

The SJC has long read these as **two distinct taxing powers**: taxes on persons/property must be **proportional *and* reasonable**, whereas **duties and excises on commodities need only be *reasonable* (not proportional/uniform).** The sales/use tax is the paradigmatic commodity excise. **Implication:** any theory premised on *non-uniformity* or *disproportionality* of the tax is a doctrinal mismatch, because excises are constitutionally exempt from the proportionality command.

### A. Clause-by-clause evaluation

| Clause | Doctrinal test | Type of claim it supports | Fit for this challenge |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Part 2, c.1, §1, Art. 4 — excise must be "reasonable"** | Whether the excise is *reasonable* (deferential); proportionality **not** required for excises | Facial challenge that the excise is unreasonable/arbitrary | **Strongest categorical fit** — it is the *only* clause that directly governs a commodity excise. But a flat 6.25% statewide rate is presumptively "reasonable," so the same clause is the State's best defense. |
| **Decl. of Rights Art. 1 (as amended by Art. 106 ERA) — acquiring/protecting property; equality** | Rational-basis-type review; equal treatment | Equal-protection-style or property-rights facial claim | Weak/uncertain. A generally applicable, content-neutral excise on goods is the easy case for rational basis; no suspect classification or fundamental right is burdened. |
| **Decl. of Rights Art. 10 — consent to taxation; no taking without consent of "the representative body of the people"; reasonable compensation for takings** | (i) *Consent* prong satisfied by duly enacted legislation; (ii) *takings* prong requires a "taking" of property for public use | Consent-to-tax theory; takings theory | Consent prong is **satisfied** by legislative enactment (representational consent), so it does not bar a validly passed tax. Takings prong is a **non-fit** for the *imposition* of a tax (see Tyler line below). |
| **Decl. of Rights Art. 12 — due process** | Procedural/substantive due process | Process or arbitrariness claim | Weak as a freestanding hook; collapses into "reasonableness" under Art. 4. |
| **Amend. Art. 44 — income-tax uniformity (flat-rate command for income)** | Income must be taxed "at a uniform rate" | Challenge to *graduated income* taxation | **Non-fit.** Art. 44 governs the *income* tax only. The leading cases — *Opinion of the Justices*, 383 Mass. 940 (1981); *Aronson v. Commonwealth*, 401 Mass. 244 (1987) — are income-tax decisions and do **not** reach a sales/commodity excise. |
| **Amend. Art. 112 — classification of *real property* for local taxation** | Authorizes classified property-tax rates | Property-tax classification disputes | Non-fit; concerns local real-property taxation, not a state commodity excise. |
| **Fed. Takings (U.S. Const. amend. V) / *Tyler* line** | Government may not retain value beyond the tax debt | Surplus-retention/home-equity-theft takings | **Non-fit for imposition.** Applies only where the State *keeps more than it is owed*, not where it levies a lawful tax. |

### B. Representative precedent (with parentheticals)

- **Opinion of the Justices to the House of Representatives, 383 Mass. 940 (1981)** (advisory opinion; graduated income-tax proposal would violate Art. 44's uniformity command — illustrates that *uniformity* doctrine lives in the **income** context, not the excise context).
- **Aronson v. Commonwealth, 401 Mass. 244 (1987)** (differential Part A (10%) / Part B (5%) income-tax rates; rejected Art. 44 and equal-protection attacks but remanded a commerce-clause claim — an *income*-tax case; not controlling for a commodity excise).
- **VAS Holdings & Investments LLC v. Commissioner of Revenue, 489 Mass. 669 (2022)** (gain on sale of an in-state LLC interest; SJC held the gain *could* be taxed consistent with constitutional limits, but the Commissioner *lacked statutory authority* — illustrates the SJC's frequent disposition of tax challenges on **statutory** rather than constitutional grounds, and its analysis of constitutional limits on the State's reach).
- **Anderson v. Attorney General, 479 Mass. 780 (2018)** ("*Anderson I*"; struck the millionaire's-tax initiative petition on Art. 48 "related subjects" grounds) and **Anderson v. Attorney General, SJC-13257 (2022)** ("*Anderson II*"; approved the AG's ballot summary) — *income*-surtax/constitutional-amendment cases; relevant as **structural illustrations** of how Massachusetts changes its tax-uniformity rules (by constitutional amendment), underscoring that the *income*-uniformity doctrine is amendment-specific and not transferable to excises.
- **Tyler v. Hennepin County, 598 U.S. 631 (2023)** (unanimous; retention of surplus equity above the tax debt in a tax-foreclosure sale is a Fifth Amendment taking) and **Foss v. City of New Bedford, No. 1:22-cv-10761 (D. Mass.)** (Massachusetts home-equity-theft/tax-foreclosure litigation in the *Tyler* line) — **tax-*takings*** authority. *Important framing:* these concern the **post-collection retention of surplus value**, not the **imposition** of a sales tax, and therefore do **not** supply a hook against levying the 6.25% tax. They are included because the prompt references them; counsel should be candid that they are off-point for the imposition theory.
- **Exhaustion/forum:** *DiStefano v. Commissioner of Revenue*, 394 Mass. 315 (1985); *ACE Property & Casualty Ins. Co. v. Commissioner of Revenue*, 437 Mass. 241 (2002); *DeMoranville v. Commissioner of Revenue*, 457 Mass. 30 (2010).
- **Standing:** *Doe v. Governor*, 381 Mass. 702 (1980); *Slama v. Attorney General*, 384 Mass. 620 (1981).

### C. Which clause is the strongest categorical fit — and why

**Part 2, c. 1, § 1, Art. 4's "reasonable ... excises upon ... commodities" clause is the strongest categorical fit**, because it is the *only* constitutional provision that actually governs the kind of tax at issue (a commodity excise). Every other candidate is a category error: Art. 44 and the uniformity cases are about the *income* tax; Art. 112 is about *real-property* classification; the *Tyler/Foss* takings line is about *retaining surplus equity*, not levying a tax; Arts. 1/10/12 reduce, in the excise setting, to a deferential reasonableness inquiry that merges back into Art. 4.

The honest corollary (stated, not argued): because Art. 4 demands only **reasonableness** — not proportionality or uniformity — and a flat statewide 6.25% rate applied to goods generally is the textbook "reasonable" excise, **the strongest doctrinal hook and the State's strongest defense are the same clause.** There is no Massachusetts constitutional provision that *categorically forbids* taxing home-construction materials. The viable *pathway* is procedural and standing-driven (Parts 2–3); the constitutional *merits* hook is narrow and faces the reasonableness presumption head-on.

### D. Candid list of weakest points / likely State defenses (identified, not argued)

1. **Excise ≠ proportional/uniform.** The decisive obstacle: under Part 2, c.1, §1, Art. 4 a commodity excise need only be **reasonable**, and a **flat 6.25% statewide rate** is the archetype of a reasonable excise. Uniformity/proportionality theories are category errors here.
2. **No clause categorically bars taxing construction materials.** There is no Massachusetts constitutional provision that singles out home-construction materials for protection; the Legislature's broad excise power (Art. 4) is the strongest defense.
3. **Exhaustion.** The State will move to dismiss the c. 231A action under § 3 and *DiStefano/ACE Property/DeMoranville*, arguing the ATB is the exclusive/preferred forum and the "narrow" exception is unmet. Outcome is genuinely uncertain and framing-dependent.
4. **Art. 44 / uniformity cases are inapposite.** *Opinion of the Justices* (383 Mass. 940) and *Aronson* are income-tax authority and do not transfer to a sales/commodity excise.
5. **Takings line is off-point.** *Tyler/Foss* address surplus retention in tax foreclosures, not the imposition of a tax; the State will argue (correctly) that paying a lawful excise is not a compensable "taking."
6. **No existing owner-builder exemption — and the 2026 measure cuts both ways.** The Legislature's new, *capped/targeted* multifamily exemption demonstrates the very line-drawing courts defer to in the excise context (rational-basis/reasonableness), and its deliberate omission of single-family owner-builders is a legislative policy choice, not a constitutional defect.
7. **Consent-to-tax (Art. 10) is satisfied by enactment.** The "no taking without consent ... of the representative body of the people" clause is met by the duly enacted statute; it is not a veto over legislative taxation.
8. **Rational basis on any equal-protection/Art. 1 framing.** A generally applicable excise on goods comfortably survives rational-basis review absent a suspect class or fundamental right.
