## 0. Scope, assumptions, and cautions

**What this memo does.** It maps the *legal pathway* for challenging the *imposition* of the 6.25% Massachusetts sales/use tax on construction materials bought by an individual acting as their own general contractor, as a *categorical/facial* matter under the Massachusetts Constitution. It addresses tax mechanics, procedural vehicle, standing, and the candidate constitutional clauses. Per instruction, it does **not** handicap the odds of success; it assesses *doctrinal fit* and *posture* and flags where authority is unsettled or adverse.

**Revision note (this version).** At the client's direction, the constitutional analysis (Part 4) is now built around the **Massachusetts Declaration of Rights guarantees of the right to property** — Article 1 (the natural right of "acquiring, possessing, and protecting property," as amended by Article 106), Article 10 (protection of property "according to standing laws," the consent-to-taxation principle, and the just-compensation clause), Article 12 (due process / "law of the land"), and Article 23 (no tax "without the consent of the people, or their representatives in the legislature"). These property clauses are the *lead* hook; the Part 2, c.1, §1, Art. 4 excise-"reasonableness" clause is retained as an *interlocking* limit (a confiscatory or unreasonable exaction that impairs the property right), and the income-uniformity (Art. 44) and surplus-retention takings (*Tyler/Foss*) theories are addressed as non-fits. The memo develops the property theory rigorously while candidly identifying the central doctrinal tension it must overcome (Arts. 10 and 23 themselves authorize taxation by legislative consent; economic/property regulation draws deferential review).

## 1. TAX MECHANICS

### A. The "contractor-as-consumer" rule (baseline)

Massachusetts imposes a 6.25% sales tax on retail sales of tangible personal property (G.L. c. 64H, § 2) and a complementary use tax at the same rate on the storage, use, or other consumption of such property in the Commonwealth (G.L. c. 64I, § 2). The use tax backstops the sales tax for untaxed/out-of-state purchases, with a credit for sales tax lawfully paid to another State (TIR 03-1).

For construction, the controlling administrative rule treats a **contractor as the consumer** of the building materials it incorporates into real property. Because the contractor is deemed to "consume" the materials in performing a real-property contract (rather than reselling tangible personal property to the owner), the *contractor* pays sales/use tax on its **cost of materials**, and the subsequent transfer of the finished improvement to the owner is not itself a taxable retail sale. See:
- **DOR Directive 02-16** (construing "building materials and supplies" and "construction vehicles, equipment and machinery" under G.L. c. 64H, § 6(f));
- **Letter Ruling 80-44** ("Materials Purchased by Construction Contractor" — materials are taxable to the contractor as consumer);
- **830 CMR 64H.1.1** (Service Enterprises) (treating contractors performing real-property work as consumers of the property they install, and prescribing certificate practice).

*Binding vs. persuasive:* The statutes (c. 64H/64I) are binding. The regulation (830 CMR 64H.1.1) has the force of law if within the Commissioner's authority. Directives and Letter Rulings are the Commissioner's interpretive positions — persuasive and binding on DOR, but not on a court.

### B. How the analysis shifts for an OWNER-BUILDER

When the **owner-builder buys materials directly**, the analysis collapses to the simplest case under c. 64H/64I, and the contractor-as-consumer construct is not even needed:

- **At the register (sales tax).** The owner-builder is the retail purchaser/consumer of tangible personal property. The vendor collects 6.25% sales tax at the point of sale (G.L. c. 64H, §§ 2, 3). The owner-builder bears and pays the tax directly.
- **Untaxed/out-of-state (use tax).** For materials bought out of state or otherwise without Massachusetts tax, the owner-builder owes **use tax** at 6.25% (G.L. c. 64I, § 2) and is the statutory taxpayer obligated to **self-assess and remit** it.

The practical upshot: an owner-builder is, unambiguously, *the person on whom the tax is imposed* — both as the consumer paying sales tax and as the taxpayer self-assessing use tax. This is the crux of the cleaner standing posture discussed in Part 3.

### C. Existing exemptions — none reaches the individual owner-builder

- **G.L. c. 64H, § 6(f) / Form ST-5C (exempt-entity projects).** Exempts building materials and supplies consumed in construction of a building or structure owned by, or held in trust for, a governmental body or a § 501(c)(3) organization, where used exclusively for the exempt purpose. A contractor on such a project tenders Form **ST-5C** to buy materials tax-free. *This is an exempt-owner/exempt-use exemption; a private individual building a personal residence does not qualify.*
- **G.L. c. 64H, § 6(r)/(s) / Form ST-12 (manufacturing, R&D, etc.).** "Exempt use" exemptions for materials/machinery consumed in manufacturing, processing, R&D, etc. *Plainly inapplicable to residential homebuilding.*
- **No "owner-builder of a single-family home" exemption exists** anywhere in c. 64H, § 6. The statute's exemptions are owner-status or use-category driven; the personal homeowner falls into none of them.

**Conclusion (Part 1.C):** As of June 2026, no statutory or regulatory exemption shelters an individual owner-builder's purchases of materials for a single-family home.

### D. The 2026 Senate measure (~$35M construction-materials exemption)

In its FY2026 work (Senate budget / consolidated "Fair Share" supplemental package), the Senate advanced — and reporting indicates the Governor signed — a **new sales-tax exemption for building materials and supplies, capped at ~$35 million annually**, targeted at **multifamily housing production**. Key features as reported:
- Eligibility limited to **approved multifamily projects**, prioritized by the Executive Office of Housing and Livable Communities (EOHLC);
- Targeting criteria such as **≥15% affordable units** and/or location in communities below a defined income threshold;
- Projected to support tens of thousands of housing units over ~10 years.

**Critical point for the client:** The measure is a *capped, discretionary, project-based* program for *multifamily/affordable* development administered through EOHLC. By its terms it does **not** reach an **individual owner-builder constructing a single-family home for personal occupancy**. If anything, the new exemption *sharpens an equal-protection-flavored framing* (the Legislature now exempts some construction materials but not the owner-builder's), while simultaneously *cutting against* a facial challenge (it demonstrates the Legislature making precisely the line-drawing/classification choices that courts defer to in the excise context — see Part 4).

> **Verify before relying:** confirm the enacted bill's chapter/section, effective date, and final eligibility text. Treat the "signed into law" status as reported-but-unconfirmed pending a pull of the session law.

## 2. PROCEDURAL PATHWAY (core question)

### A. Facial/categorical challenge vs. as-applied dispute

- A **facial/categorical** challenge attacks the *statute itself* — the contention that the Commonwealth may not constitutionally tax this category (home-construction materials of an owner-builder) at all. Relief sought is a **declaration of invalidity** and prospective injunction, not a refund tied to specific receipts. The natural vehicle is a **declaratory judgment** under **G.L. c. 231A**.
- An **as-applied** dispute contests the tax on *particular purchases/assessments* (amount, taxability of specific items, credit for out-of-state tax). The exclusive statutory channel is **abatement → Appellate Tax Board (ATB) → appellate review**.

These two tracks have different forums, defendants, deadlines, and — critically — different exhaustion consequences. The pleading must commit to one theory of relief and frame the record accordingly (Part 2.D).

### B. The facial vehicle: G.L. c. 231A declaratory judgment

**Statutory architecture:**
- **§ 1 (jurisdiction & actual controversy).** The Supreme Judicial Court, Superior Court, Land Court, and Probate Courts may make binding declarations of right "in any case in which an *actual controversy* has arisen." The controversy must be real and definite, not hypothetical.
- **§ 2 (applicable controversies).** Expressly reaches challenges to the "validity" of a statute, ordinance, or administrative regulation — i.e., constitutional attacks on a tax statute are within the chapter's contemplated subject matter.
- **§ 3 (discretionary refusal).** The court "may refuse to render a declaratory judgment ... where such judgment ... would not terminate the uncertainty or controversy." This is the textual seat of the **exhaustion/prudential-abstention** doctrine in tax cases (Part 2.D).
- **§ 8 (necessary parties; AG notice).** All persons with an interest that would be affected must be joined; and **where a statute, ordinance, or franchise is "alleged to be unconstitutional, the attorney general shall ... be entitled to be heard."** Serve the Attorney General.
- **Mass. R. Civ. P. 57.** Confirms that the existence of another adequate remedy does not, by itself, bar declaratory relief, but the *procedure* tracks ordinary civil actions; speedy hearing may be ordered.

**Proper court.** Concurrent original jurisdiction lies in the **Superior Court** (the ordinary choice) and the **SJC** (a single justice of the SJC for Suffolk County can entertain such actions, and the full SJC may take novel constitutional tax questions of statewide importance). Filing in Superior Court preserves an appeal of right; an SJC single-justice filing positions the matter for direct SJC attention but is discretionary.

**Proper defendant.** The **Commissioner of Revenue** (the official charged with administering and collecting the tax) is the indispensable defendant. Naming the **Commonwealth** and serving the **Attorney General** (mandatory under § 8 for a constitutional attack) is prudent.

### C. The as-applied vehicle: abatement → ATB → appellate review

1. **Application for abatement to the Commissioner — G.L. c. 62C, § 37.** "Any person aggrieved by the assessment of a tax" may apply, by the later of: 3 years from return filing, 2 years from assessment, or **1 year from payment**. For a self-assessed use tax, the owner-builder files the return, pays, then applies for abatement.
2. **Appeal to the Appellate Tax Board — G.L. c. 62C, § 39.** If the Commissioner denies (or is deemed to deny) the application, the taxpayer petitions the ATB **within 60 days of the notice of denial** (or within 6 months of a deemed denial). The ATB is a quasi-judicial agency with tax expertise.
3. **Pay-to-appeal.** For self-assessed/state taxes the practical rule is **pay first**: the tax must generally be paid as a predicate to abatement and ATB jurisdiction (c. 62C, § 37 keys the limitations period to payment; the ATB's jurisdiction over a c. 62C, § 39 appeal presupposes a properly paid-and-denied abatement). Collection is stayed while a *timely* abatement/appeal is pending, but the jurisdictional predicate for the ATB is the paid-and-denied abatement.
4. **Appellate review — G.L. c. 58A, § 13.** From a final ATB decision, "an appeal as to matters of law may be taken to the **Appeals Court**" (and thence to the SJC by further appellate review). Section 13 makes this "the exclusive method of reviewing any action of the board." Note that small-claims-procedure ATB decisions (c. 58A, § 7B) are **not** judicially reviewable — so do **not** elect small claims if appellate review of a legal question is the goal.

### D. The EXHAUSTION question — when § 3 forces the claim into the ATB track, and how to plead around it

**The default rule is adverse.** Massachusetts courts treat the abatement/ATB process as the **preferred and ordinarily exclusive** route for tax disputes, and will decline declaratory relief under c. 231A, § 3 where an adequate administrative remedy exists:
- **DiStefano v. Commissioner of Revenue, 394 Mass. 315, 321 (1985)** — "the Appellate Tax Board is the preferred forum for resolving questions of this type because of its special expertise in tax matters."
- **ACE Property & Casualty Ins. Co. v. Commissioner of Revenue, 437 Mass. 241, 243–244 (2002)** — declaratory relief in tax matters is confined to a "narrow set of circumstances" that excuse the preliminary exhaustion of administrative remedies.
- **DeMoranville v. Commissioner of Revenue, 457 Mass. 30, 34 (2010)** — reaffirming the exhaustion preference and its limits.

**The recognized exception** — the lane the client must occupy — applies where the question is:
1. **a pure question of law** (no material factual dispute about the transactions),
2. **of broad/recurrent public significance**, and
3. one whose resolution would **terminate the controversy** (231A, § 1/§ 3).

**Pleading framework to keep a genuine facial challenge in Superior Court/SJC:**
- **Frame the relief as categorical, not transactional.** Seek a declaration that c. 64H/64I, *as applied to the category of owner-builder home-construction materials*, violates a specified constitutional clause — not a refund of itemized receipts. A refund prayer invites a § 3 dismissal into abatement.
- **Stipulate away the facts.** Plead that there is *no dispute* the client paid the tax / owes the use tax; the only question is the statute's constitutional validity. This removes any "ATB fact-finding expertise" rationale.
- **Plead the public-significance and recurrence elements expressly** (the tax recurs on every owner-builder; the question is one of general statewide importance — reinforced, ironically, by the Legislature's 2026 attention to construction-materials taxation).
- **Name the Commissioner and serve the Attorney General** (§ 8), and pray that the declaration would "terminate the controversy."
- **Belt-and-suspenders:** Because the exception is narrow and its application uncertain, *preserve the as-applied track in parallel* — file a protective abatement under c. 62C, § 37 within the limitations window so the claim is not time-barred if the c. 231A action is dismissed under § 3. The two are not mutually exclusive; a court may even stay one pending the other.

> **Unsettled:** Whether a court will treat *this* challenge as fitting the ACE Property "narrow set of circumstances" is genuinely uncertain and fact/framing dependent. The stronger the facial/pure-law framing and the public-significance showing, the better the odds of staying in Superior Court/SJC; any reliance on disputed transaction facts pushes the case into the ATB.

## 3. STANDING

### A. The elements — federal frame and the Massachusetts "aggrieved person" standard

Massachusetts has no Article III "case or controversy" clause, but its standing doctrine is functionally similar and is operationalized through (i) c. 231A's **"actual controversy"** requirement and (ii) the recurring statutory phrase **"person aggrieved."** The three familiar elements map cleanly:
- **Injury-in-fact** — a concrete, particularized, non-speculative harm. Massachusetts requires a "real and specific" individual grievance and rejects abstract/generalized complaints. See **Doe v. Governor, 381 Mass. 702 (1980)** (complaint defective for failing to allege injury in fact to a specific, aggrieved individual); **Slama v. Attorney General, 384 Mass. 620 (1981)** (standing turns on a concrete stake; a party without a particularized interest lacks standing).
- **Causation/traceability** — the injury must flow from the challenged tax statute.
- **Redressability** — a declaration of invalidity (and refund/cessation) would remedy the injury.

For the **administrative track**, the statutory standing key is **"any person aggrieved by the assessment of a tax"** (G.L. c. 62C, § 37). The taxpayer who paid (or self-assessed) the tax is the paradigmatic aggrieved person.

### B. Why the OWNER-BUILDER has cleaner standing than a homeowner who hires a contractor

This is the single most important structural point:
- **Owner-builder (direct taxpayer).** The owner-builder *is* the consumer who pays sales tax at the register and *is* the statutory taxpayer who self-assesses and remits use tax. The injury is direct, concrete, out-of-pocket, and traceable to the statute. The owner-builder is squarely a "person aggrieved" under c. 62C, § 37, and has an "actual controversy" under c. 231A, § 1.
- **Homeowner who hires a contractor (pass-through).** Under the contractor-as-consumer rule (Part 1.A), the **contractor**, not the homeowner, is the taxpayer; the homeowner bears the tax only *economically*, embedded in the contract price. That homeowner is **not** the person on whom the tax is imposed and is generally **not** a "person aggrieved" entitled to seek abatement — the classic **pass-through/indirect-payer standing bar**. Only the contractor (the statutory taxpayer) could pursue abatement, and the contractor typically lacks incentive to litigate the homeowner's constitutional theory.

The owner-builder thus *cures the standing defect* that would sink a hire-a-contractor homeowner. The recommended posture — owner-builder **self-assessing use tax** and then challenging — produces the cleanest possible plaintiff: a named taxpayer, a quantified payment, a direct statutory obligation.

### C. Supporting authority and the general-grievance limit

- **Taxpayer/aggrieved standing:** Doe v. Governor and Slama (supra) anchor the "real and specific injury" requirement; c. 62C, § 37's "person aggrieved" supplies the statutory hook for the direct payer.
- **General-grievance limit:** A plaintiff complaining only as a taxpayer-qua-citizen about how public money is taxed/spent, without a particularized injury, generally lacks standing (the Doe v. Governor "specific grievance" rule). The owner-builder avoids this by pleading their *own* tax payments, not a generalized objection to the sales tax as a citizen.

## 4. CONSTITUTIONAL HOOK — THE STATE RIGHT TO PROPERTY (LEAD THEORY)

This challenge is anchored on the **Massachusetts Declaration of Rights guarantee of the right to property.** The theory is that the tax power, though textually broad, is bounded by the people's reserved property right, and that taxing the very materials by which an individual exercises the core property right — acquiring, building, and possessing a home — at minimum demands a real and substantial public justification rather than reflexive revenue convenience. The analysis below builds that hook, then situates the excise-power clause as an interlocking limit and confronts, candidly, the central tension the theory must overcome.

### A. The property-rights cluster (the lead hook)

Four provisions of Part the First operate together as the Commonwealth's property guarantee:

> **Art. 1 (as amended by Art. 106, 1976).** "All people are born free and equal and have certain natural, essential and unalienable rights; among which may be reckoned ... that of *acquiring, possessing, and protecting property* ...."
>
> **Art. 10.** "Each individual of the society has a right to be protected by it in the *enjoyment of his life, liberty and property, according to standing laws*. He is obliged, consequently, to *contribute his share to the expense of this protection* ....But no part of the property of any individual can, with justice, be *taken from him, or applied to public uses, without his own consent, or that of the representative body of the people* ....And whenever the public exigencies require that the property of any individual should be appropriated to public uses, he shall receive a *reasonable compensation* therefor."
>
> **Art. 12.** No subject shall be "deprived of his property ... but by the judgment of his peers, or the law of the land" (the Massachusetts due-process / "law of the land" guarantee).
>
> **Art. 23.** "No subsidy, charge, tax, impost, or duties, ought to be established, fixed, laid, or levied, under any pretext whatsoever, *without the consent of the people, or their representatives in the legislature*."

**What the cluster supplies.** Art. 1 establishes property acquisition and protection as a *natural, enumerated* right (not merely an unenumerated liberty interest). Art. 10 is the Commonwealth's principal source of substantive and procedural **due-process** protection for property, *and* it houses both the consent-to-taxation principle and the just-compensation command. Art. 12 reinforces the due-process limit. Art. 23 is the textual taxation-consent clause ("no taxation without representation"). Together they frame the constitutional proposition the client advances: the property right is a *reserved* right against which every exercise of the taxing power must be measured.

### B. The doctrinal theory built on the property right

1. **The right and its core application.** The right to "acquire, possess, and protect property" (Art. 1) is most concretely exercised in building and owning a home. A tax imposed on the *inputs* to that act (lumber, fixtures, supplies) is a direct charge on the exercise of the enumerated right, not an incidental regulation of commerce. The framing positions the home — and the act of homebuilding — at the center of the protected interest.
2. **The operative limits drawn from Art. 10.** Art. 10 protects enjoyment of property "according to standing laws" and authorizes appropriation of property to public uses only on (a) consent and (b) "reasonable compensation." The property-based challenge proceeds on the theory that an exaction that *functions* as an uncompensated appropriation of property — rather than a true, generally borne contribution to "the expense of this protection" — exceeds the boundary Art. 10 itself sets between the *tax power* and the *taking* of property.
3. **The standard of review (be precise).** Massachusetts reviews economic and property regulation under a **rational-basis** standard rooted in Arts. 1, 10, and 12: the measure must "bear[] a real and substantial relation to the public health, safety, morals, or some other phase of the general welfare." *Goodridge v. Department of Public Health*, 440 Mass. 309, 330 (2003), quoting *Coffee-Rich, Inc. v. Commissioner of Pub. Health*, 348 Mass. 414, 422 (1965). The property-rights theory must therefore either (a) operate *within* rational-basis review — arguing that taxing the materials of a personal residence lacks a real and substantial relation to a legitimate public end (a demanding showing), or (b) argue that the burden on a *natural, enumerated* property right (Art. 1) and on the home warrants review more searching than ordinary economic rational basis. Massachusetts has *not* recognized heightened scrutiny for a generally applicable excise touching property, so framing (b) is **a request to extend doctrine**, expressly flagged as such.
4. **The public-purpose limit.** Independent of rate, Massachusetts taxation must serve a **public purpose**; a levy that in substance transfers private property without a public end is not a valid tax. This is the most natural property-rights *merits* argument: not that the rate is too high, but that taxing an individual's own home-building inputs is not a constitutionally sufficient public use of that individual's property under Art. 10. *(Authority on the public-purpose requirement runs through the Opinions of the Justices line; confirm and pinpoint-cite before filing.)*
5. **The compensation strand (Art. 10) and the *Tyler/Foss* connection.** Art. 10's just-compensation clause and the federal *Tyler* line share a common principle: the government may not, under the guise of a revenue measure, appropriate more of an individual's property than the public claim supports. *Tyler* and *Foss* arose in tax-*foreclosure surplus* settings, so they are **analogous, not on-all-fours**, for an imposition challenge; their value here is as persuasive support for the structural premise that the property right cabins the revenue power — not as controlling authority that a sales tax is a "taking."

### C. The central tension the property theory must overcome (stated candidly, not argued away)

The property clauses cut **both** ways, and the challenge must meet this head-on:
- **Art. 10 and Art. 23 themselves authorize taxation.** Art. 10 expressly obliges each individual to "contribute his share to the expense of this protection," and both Art. 10 and Art. 23 permit property to be applied to public uses / taxes to be levied **with the consent of "the representative body of the people."** The sales/use tax was duly enacted by the Legislature; the *consent* condition is therefore facially satisfied. The property right, in other words, is textually *reconciled* with the tax power in the very same clauses — so the theory cannot be that taxation per se violates the property right, but only that *this* exaction exceeds the tax power's boundary (confiscation / absence of public purpose / want of real-and-substantial relation).
- **Deferential review.** Under *Goodridge / Coffee-Rich*, a generally applicable revenue measure carries a strong presumption of validity. This is the principal doctrinal headwind for framing (a) and the reason framing (b) is candidly labeled an extension.

### D. Interlocking and secondary clauses

- **Part 2, c. 1, § 1, Art. 4 — the excise power ("reasonable ... duties and excises upon ... commodities").** The sales/use tax is a **commodity excise**, which need only be **reasonable** (not "proportional and reasonable," the standard for property/poll taxes). In the property-centered framing, Art. 4 is **interlocking**: an excise that is *confiscatory* or *unreasonable* as applied to the materials of a personal home is both an invalid excise (Art. 4) *and* an impairment of the property right (Arts. 1, 10). The honest caveat: a flat 6.25% statewide rate is the textbook "reasonable" excise, so Art. 4 reinforces but does not rescue the property theory.
- **Amend. Art. 44 (income-tax uniformity)** — **Non-fit.** Governs only the *income* tax; the leading cases (*Opinion of the Justices*, 383 Mass. 940; *Aronson v. Commonwealth*, 401 Mass. 244) are income-tax decisions and do not transfer to a commodity excise.
- **Amend. Art. 112 (classification of *real property* for local taxation)** — **Non-fit;** concerns local real-property taxation, not a state excise.
- **Equal-protection framing (Arts. 1, 10).** The 2026 multifamily exemption (Part 1.D) creates a line — exempt construction materials for some projects, taxed for the single-family owner-builder. This supports an *equal-protection-flavored* property argument, but it draws the same deferential rational-basis review and the Legislature's line is the kind courts ordinarily uphold.

### E. Representative precedent (with parentheticals)

- **Goodridge v. Department of Public Health, 440 Mass. 309 (2003)** (articulates the Massachusetts substantive-due-process/rational-basis framework under Arts. 1, 10, 12 — the controlling **standard of review** for the property-rights claim).
- **Coffee-Rich, Inc. v. Commissioner of Pub. Health, 348 Mass. 414, 422 (1965)** (source of the "real and substantial relation to ... the general welfare" formulation of rational basis for economic/property regulation).
- **Opinion of the Justices to the House of Representatives, 383 Mass. 940 (1981)** and **Aronson v. Commonwealth, 401 Mass. 244 (1987)** (*income*-tax/uniformity decisions — included to show the uniformity doctrine is amendment-specific and **does not** carry the property theory; non-fit).
- **VAS Holdings & Investments LLC v. Commissioner of Revenue, 489 Mass. 669 (2022)** (SJC analyzes constitutional limits on the State's reach but resolves on **statutory** grounds — illustrates the SJC's preference for statutory dispositions and its treatment of constitutional restraints on taxation).
- **Tyler v. Hennepin County, 598 U.S. 631 (2023)** and **Foss v. City of New Bedford, No. 1:22-cv-10761 (D. Mass.)** (tax-*foreclosure surplus* takings — **persuasive/analogous** support for the structural premise that the property right cabins the revenue power; *not* on-all-fours for an imposition challenge).
- **Exhaustion/forum:** *DiStefano v. Commissioner of Revenue*, 394 Mass. 315 (1985); *ACE Property & Casualty Ins. Co. v. Commissioner of Revenue*, 437 Mass. 241 (2002); *DeMoranville v. Commissioner of Revenue*, 457 Mass. 30 (2010).
- **Standing:** *Doe v. Governor*, 381 Mass. 702 (1980); *Slama v. Attorney General*, 384 Mass. 620 (1981).

### F. Which clause is the strongest fit — and why (property-centered)

**Declaration of Rights Art. 10, read with Art. 1, is the strongest fit for a property-rights theory**, for three reasons: (i) it is the Commonwealth's principal textual guarantee of the *enjoyment and protection of property* and its chief due-process source; (ii) it uniquely contains *both* the property-protection guarantee and the boundary language (consent + "reasonable compensation" for appropriation to "public uses") that lets counsel argue an exaction has crossed from *tax* into *taking*; and (iii) it pairs naturally with Art. 1's enumerated right to "acquire, possess, and protect property" and with Art. 4's "reasonable" excise limit to frame a confiscatory/no-public-purpose exaction as simultaneously an invalid excise and an impairment of property. Art. 23 supplies the consent backdrop, and Art. 12 the due-process backstop.

**The candid corollary (stated, not argued):** the same Art. 10/Art. 23 text that anchors the right also authorizes legislatively consented taxation and obliges the individual to "contribute his share," and *Goodridge/Coffee-Rich* supply only deferential rational-basis review for a generally applicable excise. The property hook is therefore strongest as a **public-purpose / confiscation** theory (the tax exceeds the boundary the property right sets on the revenue power) and weakest if framed as a demand for heightened scrutiny, which would require the SJC to **extend** existing doctrine. This memo identifies that fit and that obstacle; per instruction it does not handicap the outcome.

### D. Candid list of weakest points / likely State defenses (identified, not argued)

1. **The property clauses authorize the tax (the central tension).** Art. 10 obliges each individual to "contribute his share to the expense of [governmental] protection," and Arts. 10 and 23 permit taxation/appropriation to public uses **with the consent of "the representative body of the people."** The sales/use tax is a duly enacted statute, so the consent condition is facially met. The State will argue the property right is *reconciled with* — not a veto on — legislatively consented taxation. The theory must therefore prove this exaction exceeds the tax power's boundary, not that taxation per se offends property.
2. **Deferential review.** Under *Goodridge / Coffee-Rich*, a generally applicable revenue measure need only "bear a real and substantial relation to ... the general welfare." Raising revenue and treating homebuilding inputs like other taxable goods easily clears that bar; heightened scrutiny for an excise touching property is not established MA doctrine, so the strongest framing asks the SJC to **extend** doctrine.
3. **Excise need only be "reasonable."** Under Part 2, c.1, §1, Art. 4 a commodity excise need not be proportional/uniform, and a **flat 6.25% statewide rate** is the archetype of a "reasonable" excise — limiting the interlocking Art. 4 argument.
4. **Public-purpose argument is uphill.** Revenue generation is itself a recognized public purpose; the State will say nothing about taxing construction materials lacks a public end, so the "no public purpose / confiscation" theory must show the exaction is *in substance* an appropriation, not a tax.
5. **Exhaustion.** The State will move to dismiss the c. 231A action under § 3 and *DiStefano / ACE Property / DeMoranville*, arguing the ATB is the preferred/exclusive forum and the "narrow" exception is unmet. Outcome is genuinely uncertain and framing-dependent.
6. **Takings line is not on-all-fours.** *Tyler/Foss* address *surplus retention* in tax foreclosures, not the imposition of a tax; the State will argue paying a lawful excise is not a compensable "taking." They support only the structural premise.
7. **No existing owner-builder exemption — and the 2026 measure cuts both ways.** The new *capped/targeted* multifamily exemption illustrates the line-drawing courts defer to under rational basis; its omission of single-family owner-builders is a legislative policy choice, not a constitutional defect.
8. **Art. 44 / uniformity cases are inapposite.** *Opinion of the Justices* (383 Mass. 940) and *Aronson* are income-tax authority and do not transfer to a commodity excise — so they neither help nor (the State will say) constrain the analysis.

## 6. Missing facts that would change the analysis

- **Use-tax self-assessment.** Whether the client has actually filed/paid use tax (vs. only paying sales tax at the register) affects the cleanest standing framing and the abatement limitations clock.
- **Resale/development intent.** If the home is for resale or the client is functioning as a developer, the analysis (and possibly the 2026 multifamily exemption's reach) changes materially.
- **Amounts and timeline.** Dates of payment drive the c. 62C, § 37 limitations windows and the c. 62C, § 39 / c. 58A, § 13 appeal deadlines.
- **Out-of-state purchases and credits.** TIR 03-1 credit for tax paid to other States may reduce or reframe the use-tax injury.
- **Final text of the 2026 exemption.** Confirm chapter/section, effective date, and eligibility before relying on its scope or using it for an equal-protection-flavored framing.
- **Public-purpose / confiscation record.** The property-rights merits theory (Part 4.B.4–5) turns on facts about the tax's burden relative to a public end — e.g., the aggregate tax cost as a share of total home-construction cost, and any legislative record bearing on purpose. Develop this record to support the public-purpose/confiscation framing.
- **Whether to seek an extension of doctrine.** A decision is needed on whether to argue (a) the tax fails *rational basis* as applied (works within current law) or (b) the burden on the enumerated Art. 1 property right and the home warrants *heightened* review (asks the SJC to extend doctrine). The framings can be pleaded in the alternative but should be a deliberate strategic choice.
