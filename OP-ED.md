# I'm Building My Family's House, and the State Charges Me for Every Board

### The sales tax treats the materials of a home like any other purchase. The constitution John Adams wrote for this state suggests it shouldn't.

**By [Author Name]**

---

Last year I became my own general contractor, which is a generous way of saying I decided to build my family's house myself instead of handing the job to someone with a bigger crew and a bigger markup. I am not a developer and I have nothing to flip. I am a person trying to get my kids under a roof we can actually afford, and over the past several months I have learned that the Commonwealth regards that ambition as a long sequence of taxable purchases.

The mechanics are simple enough. Massachusetts adds 6.25 percent to nearly everything that goes into a house. Buy framing lumber or a window or a spool of wire from a supplier here, and the tax is right there on the receipt. Order something from out of state to save a little money, and you owe the same rate as a "use tax" you are expected to report on yourself. Not one of these things is a luxury. You cannot raise a wall without them, and by the time my family moves in, the tax on the raw materials alone will have cost us thousands of dollars.

I want to be fair, because I am not someone who thinks taxation is theft. I drive the roads that carried every one of those deliveries. My children will go to the public school a few streets over. If the place ever catches fire, I will be grateful the town sends a truck. Paying for what we hold in common is the cost of living somewhere worth living, and I have never begrudged it.

But contributing to the common account is a different thing from being charged for the act of making a home, and that distinction is older than our tax code. It is older than the country. When John Adams sat down in 1780 to draft the Massachusetts constitution we still operate under, he placed two ideas near the very front of it. The first was that people hold a natural right to acquire and protect property. The second was that no tax should fall on them "without the consent of the people, or their representatives." He wrote those words because he and his neighbors had just spent a decade discovering how it feels to be taxed by a government that never thought to ask. The home was the thing you guarded against that kind of reach, not the thing you surrendered to it.

If that strikes you as a stretch, look at what our own Legislature did last year. It created an exemption, reportedly worth something like thirty-five million dollars annually, that lifts this exact tax off building materials for qualifying multifamily housing projects. I happen to think that is sensible policy. But it concedes the entire point. The state has now acknowledged that it can spare construction materials from the tax whenever it judges the purpose worthy, and that the budget will survive the gesture. It simply decided a developer's lumber earns the break and that mine does not, though both come off the same truck.

Talk to almost any contractor and you will hear a version of this, because the state's own rules treat the builder as the "consumer" of whatever he installs. He pays the tax and works it into his price, so the charge reaches the family no matter who swings the hammer. Hire the job out or do it yourself, as I am, and the price of a home in Massachusetts quietly carries a tax on the home's own materials.

Some will say this is a small thing to raise a fuss over, and in dollars they may be right. Yet the smallest charges tend to be the ones nobody bothers to question, which is precisely why a few of us ought to. My quarrel was never really with the size of the bill. It is with the idea that building a place to live is just another transaction the state may tax at the threshold.

I have asked a court to weigh whether taxing the materials of a family's own house can be squared with the property and consent guarantees Adams set down. Perhaps the answer arrives there. Perhaps the Legislature simply extends to the rest of us the fairness it already handed apartment builders. Either way, this is not the private grievance of a few people stubborn enough to build their own walls. Nearly everyone in this state eventually pays to build or repair a place to live, and every one of them pays this tax without ever being asked.

I did not go looking for this argument. I picked up a nail gun, and it came with the lumber.
