# I'm Building My Family a Home. The State Taxes Me for the Privilege.

### A 6.25 percent tax on the lumber and nails of a house is a tax on shelter itself — and it runs against a principle older than the Commonwealth.

**By [Author Name]**

---

This spring I poured the foundation of the house my family will live in. I am not a developer. I am not building to sell. I am one person, acting as my own general contractor, trying to put a roof over the people I love — and I am learning, one receipt at a time, that the Commonwealth of Massachusetts treats that act as a taxable event.

Every load of lumber, every box of nails, every window and wire and length of pipe arrives with the same surcharge: 6.25 percent, the state sales tax, paid at the register or owed as use tax on anything I buy from out of state. These are not luxuries. They are not furniture or ornament. They are the structural bones of a house — shelter itself, taken apart and taxed piece by piece. By the time my family sleeps under that roof, we will have paid the state thousands of dollars for the privilege of building it.

I want to be careful about what I am and am not saying. I am not asking to dodge my share of the common load. I pay for the roads my trucks drive on, the schools my children will attend, the firefighters who would come if that new house ever caught fire. That is the bargain of living in a free society, and I keep my end of it gladly.

But there is a difference — an old and important one — between contributing to what we hold in common and being charged for the most private thing a person ever does, which is to make a home. And that difference is not something I invented. It is written into the founding charter of this Commonwealth. Our Constitution of 1780, drafted in its essential parts by John Adams, declares that every person has the natural right of "acquiring, possessing, and protecting property," and that no tax may be "levied, under any pretext whatsoever, without the consent of the people, or their representatives." Those words were written by people who had just lived through what it meant to be taxed without their leave, and who understood a home to be the very thing government exists to protect — not a thing for government to meter at the threshold.

If you think the principle is abstract, look at what the Legislature itself did last year. In 2026 it created a new exemption — capped at roughly $35 million a year — that lifts the sales tax off building materials for approved multifamily housing projects. I am glad those homes will be cheaper to build. But notice what it means: the state has decided it *can* relieve construction materials of this tax, and that doing so won't break the budget. It simply chose to do it for one kind of housing and not for the family that builds its own. The lumber is identical. The wire is identical. The line between them is not a line of principle. It is a line of convenience.

Ask the contractors who build for a living, and they'll tell you the same thing, because the tax falls on them too — under the state's own rules, a builder is the "consumer" of the materials he installs, so he pays the tax and passes it straight into the price of every house. This is not a tax on profit or speculation. It is a tax on shelter, and in the end it is paid by whoever is trying to get under a roof.

A small tax is easy to wave away. That is exactly why it deserves a second look. The most forgettable line on a receipt is precisely where a principle gets quietly sold off — a few dollars at a time, on a document nobody reads twice. The amount is not the point. The threshold is the point.

I've filed to have a court decide whether taxing the materials of a family's own home can be squared with the property and consent guarantees our founders wrote down. I think the honest answer is no. But whether the remedy comes from a courtroom or from the State House, the question belongs to all of us, because every family that ever builds or rebuilds a home in this state pays this tax at its own front door.

A home is the last place a free people should be taxed without their leave. We wrote that down once. We ought to mean it.
