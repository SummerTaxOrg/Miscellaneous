# AMICUS — Brief of Amici Curiae (General Contractors & Home Builders)

This directory holds the procedural research, the standing rationale, and the historical
argument behind the model amicus brief at [`/AMICUS-BRIEF.md`](../AMICUS-BRIEF.md).

## What this is

The repository's research memo
([`MA-owner-builder-sales-tax-constitutional-memo.md`](../MA-owner-builder-sales-tax-constitutional-memo.md))
maps a constitutional challenge to the Massachusetts sales/use tax as imposed on the
home-construction materials an individual buys to build his own house. The
[`SPEECH`](../SPEECH.md) tree gave that cause a public, patriotic voice.

**This tree supplies the cause an organized ally in court: an amicus brief filed by a
coalition of general contractors and home builders**, who support the position that the
materials of a home should not bear the sales tax — and who have a genuine, direct stake
in the question because, under the Commonwealth's *contractor-as-consumer* rule, they are
themselves the taxpayers on the materials they install.

## The client's request

> Create the equivalent of an amicus brief submitted in support of the issue, by a group of
> general contractors who support the idea that there should be no sales tax on materials
> purchased for building a home. Research proper formatting as well as content. It should
> be historically convincing as well, evoking patriotism.

## Why contractors are the right amici (not a contrivance)

This is the elegant part. Under DOR Directive 02-16, Letter Ruling 80-44, and
830 CMR 64H.1.1, a contractor who incorporates materials into real property is deemed the
**consumer** of those materials and **pays the sales/use tax on them**. So contractors are
not abstract well-wishers — **they are the statutory taxpayers on the very materials at
issue**, and the tax passes through them into the price every family pays for a home. That
gives the amici a real Mass. R. App. P. 17 "interest" and a vantage no party can supply:
they can tell the Court what the tax actually falls upon. See
[`research/02-the-amici-and-their-interest.md`](research/02-the-amici-and-their-interest.md).

## Important caveats

- **Not for filing as-is.** The brief is a *model*. Caption, docket, party names, the
  identities of the amici, and counsel are bracketed **placeholders**. The named coalition
  ("The Bay State Council of General Contractors & Home Builders") is **fictional and
  illustrative** — it is not a real organization and no real trade association has been
  represented as joining. Replace with actual, consenting amici before any use.
- **Appellate posture.** Amicus practice in Massachusetts is appellate (SJC / Appeals
  Court). The brief is captioned for the SJC and assumes the case has reached that Court
  (e.g., after an adverse Superior Court judgment in the c. 231A action, or on reservation
  and report). At the trial-court stage there is no amicus vehicle; this brief presupposes
  the appeal the memo's roadmap contemplates.
- **Citations unverified.** Every authority is carried over from the memo and **must be
  Shepardized/KeyCited**, and the 2026 session law confirmed, before filing.
- **Advocacy, not prediction.** Like the memo, the brief is candid that Articles 10 and 23
  *also authorize* consented taxation; it meets that tension head-on rather than hiding it.
  The memo remains the honest assessment of the odds; the brief is the argument at its
  most persuasive.
