# 02 — The Amici and Their Interest

An amicus brief lives or dies, as a matter of admission, on the **interest** it asserts
(Mass. R. App. P. 17(a) requires the motion to "identify the interest of the applicant").
This document explains why a coalition of general contractors is not a decorative choice
of amicus but the *strongest* available one — and how their interest is framed in the
brief.

---

## I. The contractor-as-consumer rule makes contractors the taxpayers

The decisive fact comes straight from the memo's tax mechanics (Part 1.A). Massachusetts
does **not** treat a contractor as a reseller of materials to the homeowner. Instead, under:

- **DOR Directive 02-16**,
- **Letter Ruling 80-44** ("Materials Purchased by Construction Contractor"), and
- **830 CMR 64H.1.1** (Service Enterprises),

a contractor who incorporates building materials into real property is deemed the
**consumer** of those materials. The contractor therefore **pays the sales or use tax on
the cost of the materials**, and the later transfer of the finished home to the owner is
not itself a taxable sale.

The consequence for amicus standing is powerful: **general contractors are themselves the
statutory taxpayers on the very materials the case is about.** They are not sympathetic
strangers to the controversy; they are inside it. Every contractor amicus has paid, on
every project, the identical tax the owner-builder challenges — only in the contractor's
own name rather than the owner's.

## II. Why that produces a textbook Rule 17 interest

A good amicus offers the court something the parties cannot. These amici offer two things:

1. **A direct financial stake.** They pay the tax and pass it through to the families they
   build for. A ruling on the tax's validity affects their ledgers and their clients'
   prices directly — concrete, particularized, non-abstract interest of exactly the kind
   Rule 17 looks for.
2. **A vantage no party possesses.** The owner-builder plaintiff can describe *his* house;
   the contractors can describe *the whole class of transactions* — thousands of homes,
   the same lumber and wire and glass taxed on every one. They can tell the Court, from the
   ledger rather than from argument, that what the tax reaches is not luxury or speculation
   but the structural necessities of shelter. That evidentiary-flavored vantage is the
   classic justification for letting an amicus be heard.

## III. The alignment is genuine, and the brief says so

There is an honest logical point worth noting: if the tax on the owner-builder's materials
is unconstitutional, the same reasoning presses against the contractor-as-consumer tax the
amici themselves pay. The amici therefore support the plaintiff **in their own interest as
well as his** — and the brief does not hide this. It frames the principle as a single one
("the constitutional principle that governs the one governs the other"), which both
strengthens the interest statement and avoids any appearance that the amici are feigning
disinterest. Under Rule 17(c)(5), amici need not be neutral; they need only be the genuine
authors and funders of their own brief, which the declaration affirms.

## IV. Who the amici are (illustrative — replace before use)

The brief names a **fictional, illustrative** coalition so that no real organization is
represented as having joined:

- **The Bay State Council of General Contractors & Home Builders** — a nonprofit
  membership association (gets the Rule 17(c)(1) corporate-disclosure treatment).
- **The undersigned master builders** — individual licensed contractors and sole
  proprietors (natural persons; no corporate disclosure required).

Mixing an association with named individual builders is a deliberate, conventional move: the
association supplies breadth ("the trade"), and the individuals supply concreteness ("I have
framed these homes and paid this tax"). When the brief is made real, these are replaced with
actual consenting amici — e.g., a genuine builders' or contractors' association and a
handful of named member firms willing to be identified.

> **Caution.** Do not file under the name of any real trade association without its
> informed, written consent and its own review of the Rule 17(c)(5) declaration (authorship
> and funding). The coalition named in the model is invented for illustration only.

## V. How the interest is stated in the brief

The Statement of Interest is built in three beats, mirroring the structure of good
real-world examples:
1. **Who** — "the men and women who build the Commonwealth's homes"; the association and
   the individual builders.
2. **The concrete stake** — the contractor-as-consumer rule; "they *pay* it … and watch it
   pass, dollar for dollar, into the price a Massachusetts family must pay for a roof."
3. **What they uniquely offer the Court** — the demonstration that the tax falls on
   "shelter itself, taken apart and taxed piece by piece," and the housing-cost stakes for
   the families they serve.
