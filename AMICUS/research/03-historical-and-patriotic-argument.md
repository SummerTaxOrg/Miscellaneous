# 03 — The Historical and Patriotic Argument

The client asked that the brief be "historically convincing as well, evoking patriotism."
This document explains the argument that carries that weight, why it is *legitimate legal
argument* rather than ornament, and how patriotism is deployed within the discipline a
court filing demands. It differs deliberately from the [`SPEECH`](../../SPEECH.md) tree:
the speech invoked history *obliquely, through style*; a brief, by contrast, makes its
historical claim **openly and by citation** — that is how constitutional argument from
original meaning is properly done.

---

## I. The legitimate doctrinal vehicle: original meaning of the 1780 Declaration of Rights

The patriotic content is not smuggled in as mood. It rides on a recognized mode of
constitutional interpretation: **the original meaning of the constitutional text**. Three
facts make that mode unusually strong here.

1. **The text is unusually old and unusually personal.** The Massachusetts Constitution of
   1780 is the oldest functioning written constitution in the world, and its Declaration
   of Rights was drafted in its essential parts by **John Adams**. The property and consent
   clauses the case turns on (Arts. 1, 10, 23) are, quite literally, his words.

2. **The text answers a known grievance.** Those clauses were written by people who had
   spent the prior fifteen years contending — in this Commonwealth's own town meetings —
   against taxation imposed without their consent and against revenue power that did not
   respect the threshold of a private home. The consent-to-taxation principle of Article 23
   and the property guarantee of Article 1 are the *codification of that struggle*. That
   history is not decoration; it is evidence of what the words were understood to mean.

3. **The principle maps onto this tax.** The original concern — that a free people are taxed
   only by their own consent, and that the home is the object the law exists to protect —
   bears directly on a charge laid upon the materials of a family's home. The brief's move
   is: *give these words the weight their authors gave them.*

This is why Argument I leads the brief. The historical section is not a preamble to the
"real" argument; it **is** an argument — that the property and consent guarantees were
written to confine exactly this kind of exaction.

## II. How patriotism is deployed without forfeiting credibility

A brief that overplays patriotism loses the court. The discipline observed here:

- **Argument from the framers' understanding, not from flag-waving.** The brief invokes
  Adams and the founding era to establish *meaning*, and repeatedly returns to the text and
  to this Court's standard of review. It earns the emotional register by anchoring it in
  interpretation.
- **Candor as a credibility deposit.** The brief openly concedes the hard fact the memo
  flags (Part 4.C): Articles 10 and 23 *also authorize* consented taxation and oblige each
  person to "contribute his share." By meeting that tension directly — consent to the
  *common burden* is not consent to tax *shelter itself* — the brief shows the Court it is
  not hiding the ball, which is what licenses the more stirring passages.
- **Patriotism concentrated, not pervasive.** The elevated register is concentrated in two
  places — the opening of Argument I and the peroration of the Conclusion — and the middle
  sections (II.B, III) do disciplined doctrinal and practical work. The feeling frames the
  argument; it does not substitute for it.
- **No false comparison.** The brief does **not** liken a 6.25% sales tax to the tyranny of
  the Crown. It claims only that the *principle* the founders codified — consent, and the
  protection of the home — still governs, and still draws a boundary. That is a claim a
  court can accept without embarrassment.

## III. The relationship to the SPEECH tree (and why the two differ)

| | SPEECH | AMICUS BRIEF |
|---|---|---|
| Audience | the public | the Supreme Judicial Court |
| History invoked | **obliquely**, through cadence and form; never named | **openly**, by citation, as original-meaning evidence |
| Patriotism | the whole point; pervasive | a concentrated, disciplined instrument serving a legal claim |
| John Adams | a *stylistic* model (he drafted the words, so the voice fits) | an *authority* (he drafted the words, so the meaning binds) |
| Test of success | does it move and inspire? | does it persuade a court while remaining credible? |

The two are complementary expressions of one insight discovered in the research: **the
constitutional text at the heart of this case was written by John Adams to secure the home
and to forbid unconsented taxation.** The speech *feels* that fact; the brief *argues* it.

## IV. Honest limits (carried over from the memo)

The historical argument strengthens the *interpretive* case but does not dissolve the
doctrinal headwinds the memo identifies candidly:

- The same Articles authorize legislatively-consented taxation; the founders' consent
  principle is *satisfied in form* by a duly enacted statute, so the argument must be that
  the exaction exceeds the *boundary* the right sets, not that taxation offends property.
- Under *Goodridge* / *Coffee-Rich*, a generally applicable excise draws deferential
  rational-basis review; an originalist gloss does not by itself displace that standard.
- A flat statewide excise is the textbook "reasonable" excise under Pt. 2, c. 1, § 1,
  art. 4.

The brief's historical framing is therefore best understood as doing two things at once:
(1) supplying the interpretive case that the property/consent guarantees were meant to
confine charges on the home, and (2) supporting the *as-applied* theory that this tax, in
sparing other construction while taxing the family home, has crossed the boundary the
framers drew. It is advocacy at its most persuasive; the memo remains the candid forecast.
